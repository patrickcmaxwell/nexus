> What does it do to humans physically on a chemical level?

> [!ABSTRACT] **Grok** (2025-10-03 19:48:32)
> Media attached: audio
> Nicotine slips into your brain fast, binding to nicotinic acetylcholine receptors-those are ion channels on neurons. It opens them, letting sodium and calcium rush in, sparking electrical signals. This mimics acetylcholine, a natural neurotransmitter, but nicotine's stickier, lingering longer. It ramps up dopamine release in the reward pathway, especially in the nucleus accumbens, driving that addictive feel-good hit. It also tweaks other neurotransmitters-serotonin, norepinephrine, and GABA-altering mood, arousal, and anxiety. Physically, it spikes heart rate and blood pressure by stimulating the sympathetic nervous system, narrowing blood vessels. On a cellular level, it can increase oxidative stress in high doses, damaging DNA, but in low doses, it may enhance neuroplasticity, strengthening neural connections. Long-term, chronic use rewires dopamine pathways, making your brain crave it. Want specifics on any part?
