> Methylene blue interacts with the body chemically in a few key ways. It's a phenothiazine dye that gets reduced in cells to leucomethylene blue, a colorless compound, which then donates electrons to help convert methemoglobin back to hemoglobin, aiding oxygen transport. In Alzheimer's, it boosts mitochondrial function by acting as an electron carrier, enhancing ATP production and reducing oxidative stress by scavenging reactive oxygen species. It also inhibits nitric oxide synthase, limiting inflammation, and may bind to tau proteins, preventing their aggregation. Chemically, it forms complexes with metal ions and can cross the blood-brain barrier easily. Side effects like blue urine come from its excretion unchanged in high doses. > If you're symptom-free, taking methylene blue can still cause side effects. Common ones include nausea, dizziness, headache, or stomach upset. High doses-over fifty milligrams daily-might lead to blue-green urine or skin, which is harmless but weird. More seriously, it can cause serotonin syndrome if mixed with certain antidepressants like SSRIs, risking agitation, sweating, or worse. It might also stress kidneys or trigger allergic reactions like rashes. Long-term, we don't fully know-could mess with blood pressure or glucose levels. > Research kicked off in the late eighteen-hundreds when Paul Ehrlich used it to stain malaria parasites, hinting at its brain effects. In the nineteen-seventies, scientists noticed it boosted mitochondrial function in rats, sparking interest in neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer's. By the nineteen-nineties, studies showed it crossed the blood-brain barrier and reduced tau tangles in lab models. Human trials, like one in two thousand ten, confirmed low doses improved cognition in early Alzheimer's patients. It's been a slow build-dye to brain booster-driven by accident and curiosity.
