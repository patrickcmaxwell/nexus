> The term "oasis" does not appear explicitly in the Bible, as it is a specific English word referring to a fertile spot in a desert. However, the Bible does mention locations that scholars and translators often interpret as oases—places with water, vegetation, or rest stops in arid regions. These are typically described using terms like "springs," "wells," or specific place names. Below is a detailed look at where such references occur, based on biblical texts and their contexts, with citations from the Bible (using a standard translation like the NIV unless otherwise noted).
> 
> ### Key References to Oasis-Like Locations in the Bible
> 
> 1. **Elim (Exodus 15:27, Numbers 33:9)**  
>    - **Context**: After the Israelites crossed the Red Sea, they traveled through the Desert of Shur and arrived at Elim, described as a place with "twelve springs and seventy palm trees" where they camped near water.  
>    - **Significance**: Elim is widely regarded as an oasis, providing water and shade in the desert during the Israelites’ journey from Egypt.  
>    - **Verse (Exodus 15:27)**: "Then they came to Elim, where there were twelve springs and seventy palm trees, and they camped there near the water."  
>    - **Location**: Likely in the Sinai Peninsula, possibly near modern-day Wadi Gharandel.
> 
> 2. **Marah (Exodus 15:23–25)**  
>    - **Context**: Before reaching Elim, the Israelites arrived at Marah, where the water was bitter. God instructed Moses to throw a piece of wood into the water, making it sweet and drinkable.  
>    - **Significance**: While initially inhospitable, Marah became a life-sustaining stop, resembling an oasis after divine intervention.  
>    - **Verse (Exodus 15:23)**: "When they came to Marah, they could not drink its water because it was bitter. (That is why the place is called Marah.)"  
>    - **Location**: Possibly near modern Ain Hawarah in the Sinai.
> 
> 3. **Kadesh or Kadesh Barnea (Numbers 20:1, Deuteronomy 1:46, Numbers 13:26)**  
>    - **Context**: Kadesh, often called Kadesh Barnea, was a significant stopping point during the Israelites’ wilderness wanderings, described as having springs or water sources (e.g., the "waters of Meribah" in Numbers 20:13).  
>    - **Significance**: It served as a base for the Israelites for an extended period and is considered an oasis-like location in the Desert of Zin.  
>    - **Verse (Numbers 20:1)**: "In the first month the whole Israelite community arrived at the Desert of Zin, and they stayed at Kadesh."  
>    - **Location**: Likely near modern-day Ain el-Qudeirat in the Negev Desert.
> 
> 4. **Jericho, the "City of Palms" (Deuteronomy 34:3, Judges 1:16, 2 Chronicles 28:15)**  
>    - **Context**: Jericho is frequently associated with springs and palm trees, earning it the nickname "City of Palms." The prophet Elisha purified its spring (2 Kings 2:19–22), ensuring a reliable water source.  
>    - **Significance**: Jericho’s lush environment, supported by springs like Ain es-Sultan (Elisha’s Spring), made it a classic oasis in the arid Jordan Valley.  
>    - **Verse (Deuteronomy 34:3)**: "The Negev and the whole region from the Valley of Jericho, the City of Palms, as far as Zoar."  
>    - **Location**: Modern-day Jericho, near the Dead Sea.
> 
> 5. **En Gedi (1 Samuel 23:29, Song of Solomon 1:14)**  
>    - **Context**: En Gedi is a fertile area with springs and waterfalls in the Judean Desert, near the Dead Sea. David hid there from Saul, and it’s poetically mentioned in Song of Solomon for its vineyards and beauty.  
>    - **Significance**: A well-known oasis, En Gedi provided water and refuge in a harsh desert environment.  
>    - **Verse (Song of Solomon 1:14)**: "My beloved is to me a cluster of henna blossoms from the vineyards of En Gedi."  
>    - **Location**: Modern En Gedi, Israel, along the Dead Sea.
> 
> 6. **Springs and Wells in General (e.g., Genesis 16:7, Genesis 21:19)**  
>    - **Context**: The Bible frequently mentions wells or springs as critical water sources in desert regions, often implying oasis-like settings. For example, Hagar encountered an angel by a spring in the desert (Genesis 16:7), and God provided a well for her and Ishmael (Genesis 21:19).  
>    - **Significance**: These unnamed water sources served as life-sustaining stops for travelers, akin to oases.  
>    - **Verse (Genesis 16:7)**: "The angel of the Lord found Hagar near a spring in the desert; it was the spring that is beside the road to Shur."
> 
> ### Notes on Interpretation
> - **Translation Variations**: The word "oasis" may appear in some modern paraphrased translations (e.g., The Message), but traditional translations like the KJV, NIV, or ESV use terms like "springs," "wells," or specific place names. For example, searching a digital Bible (like BibleGateway.com) for "oasis" yields no results in the NIV, but searching "springs" or "Elim" reveals relevant passages.
> - **Geographical Context**: The Bible’s settings in the Middle East, particularly in arid regions like the Sinai, Negev, and Judean deserts, make oases critical for survival, and many place names reflect this (e.g., "En" means "spring" in Hebrew, as in En Gedi or En Rogel).
> - **Cultural Importance**: Oases were not just physical locations but also carried symbolic weight, representing God’s provision and rest (e.g., Psalm 23:2, which speaks of "still waters" in a pastoral sense).
