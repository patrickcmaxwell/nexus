>  
> ​Drug addiction costs hundreds of billions of dollars worldwide and robs people of agency, self-respect, and generativity. While rehabilitation programs are widespread, their impact is often short-lived, with many individuals relapsing after treatment. Addiction is an even greater challenge for the unhoused, whose conditions expose them to ongoing trauma and continual opportunities for relapse.
> This dissertation proposal asks: What is required to help the unhoused population achieve lasting sobriety?
> I argue that the answer lies in creating a sustainable community where unhoused individuals struggling with addiction live for up to five years. This community would provide stable housing, meaningful work that equips residents for independent livelihoods, access to weeky psychotherapy and a daily meditation practice. Additional supportive modalities will be identified through consultation with leaders of addiction recovery centers.
> The design will be organized through the Theory of Change (ToC) framework, which clarifies intended outcomes, identifies interventions, and establishes pathways of change. ToC also allows for systematic evaluation of progress should the model be implemented.

> Chapter 1: Introduction
> ​The financial cost of addiction amounts to billions of dollars (Copello et al., 2010; Harwood et al., 1998).  According to Sinha (2011), 85% of illicit drug users return to their drug(s) of choice one year after leaving a rehabilitation program. Other researchers (e.g., Kabisa et al., 2021; Mao et al., 2022; Nagy et al., 2024) have recorded relapse rates from 40%-95% depending on the population studied, length of follow-up, and the definition of relapse used by the researchers. It is beyond a doubt, however, that homelessness presents a special challenge for the addict-in-recovery, acting as an additional stressor and increasing rate of relapse significantly (Goldman Hasbun et al., 2019; Austin et al., 2021; Miller et al., 2021). Whereas there are existing aftercare options for some addicts-in-recovery (Polcin & Henderson, 2008), there are few long-term programs for unhoused addicts in recovery that address both the root systemic issues this population faces, as well as providing them with evidence-based interventions that may help them address the root of their addiction.
> Purpose Statement​
> 
> The purpose of this theoretical inquiry is to integrate both medical and alternative models for the etiology of addiction and gather interventions from both relevant databases and stores of human knowledge in the field of addiction to inform the development of a community to house the unhoused population struggling with addiction, help them recover from substance abuse and empower them to live sustainably sober lives. The central phenomenon studied by the inquiry is recovery from addiction. Following White (2007), I define recovery as an experience in which an individual who has suffered from addiction utilizes their inner and outer resources to heal the wounds that caused their addiction and leads a healthy, productive, and meaningful life.
> Brief Summary of the Literature Review
> 
> ​In surveying the literature on addiction and recovery, the central theme that emerged was one of a diversity of lenses, approaches, and interventions. While at first, I came across what seemed to be a dominant medical model of addiction (Jiang, 2024), further research into the medical paradigm revealed a multiplicity of models that sought to describe the etiology of addiction. This lack of consensus helped to underline the complexity of my subject matter, and I organized the various theories of addiction into categories. I ordered these categories roughly chronologically, beginning with indigenous models and ending with trauma-based models. Next, I looked at a multitude of factors that researchers have discovered to help addicts-in-recovery lead sustainably sober lives. These factors range from social support (Mpanza et al., 2022 & Bell et al., 2024) to mindfulness (Kabat-Zinn, 2003 & Korecki et al., 2020), and include various psychedelics (e.g., ketamine, psilocybin, and ayahuasca), which have been shown to be effective in ameliorating substance use disorder (SUD) (Jones et al., 2018; Bogenschutz & Johnson, 2016; Verres, 2014). After reviewing these various individual factors, I looked at established paths towards recovery from SUD, such as Alcoholics Anonymous and Delancey Street, highlighting both the strengths of each program as well as common critiques.
> Theoretical Perspectives
> 
> ​Tranformative theory (Mertens 1999, 2008, 2011) serves as my theoretical lens through which I will bring my project to life. Mertens’ fundamentally optimistic and activist orientation aligns with my vision for a community that can help the unhoused lead generative, purposeful lives free from substance abuse. Theory of Change (ToC) is a framework for generating compelling theories for how and why proposed interventions are effective (Weiss, 1972, 1995). Using ToC helps to sharpen the process of designing interventions, aids in measuring outcomes, and assists in demonstrating causal links between interventions and outcomes (Connell and Kubisch, 1998). Employing ToC in the planning of the Beyond Rehab Community will set up the project to move from theoretical dissertation to physical reality that might benefit the unhoused population suffering from SUD. As the movement from addiction to freedom requires a tectonic shift in self-concept, I also mined Identity Theory (Breakwell, 1983; Deaux 1993) for a deeper understanding of how we as humans source our identities and how these identities change.
> Research Problem
> 
> ​Whereas individual factors that contribute to sobriety have been studied extensively, this research has neither been compiled nor used to design a community that might help addicts live sustainably sober lives.
> Autobiographical Origins
> 
> ​Beyond Rehab is inspired by my time at the Community for the Experience and Development of Awakening and Responsibility in the summer and fall of 2019. I spent five months living a monastic schedule in which I woke together with 11 other monastics at five-thirty each morning, then practiced chanting and meditation for two hours. The cohesiveness and depth of connection that I experienced in that community was unparalleled in my previous experience.
> ​The focus on sobriety from substances comes from my own journey of recovery from addiction, specifically from cannabis. This is an addiction that has been a part of my life since my freshman year of college. I am currently abstinent, and I attribute that abstinence in part to my meditation practice. Developing a program that uses shared spiritual practice to maintain sobriety is attractive to me as it parallels my own path.
> Significance of the Study
> 
> ​Designing a community for post-rehabilitation sobriety is important for several reasons. First, the design will serve as a reference compilation of evidence-based practices that have been proven to be effective in maintaining sobriety for those who might want to design their own programs for sobriety. Second, the design can offer a template for those who might want to create their own post-rehabilitation communities for addicts-in-recovery. Third, the design will serve as the theoretical foundation for the creation of a tangible community that will serve to meet the needs of the unhoused population who struggle with SUD.
> Research Questions
> 
> My primary question is:
> What useful insights may be gleaned from relevant knowledge bases, both literary and biological, that will inform the development of a sobriety-supporting residential community for the unhoused post-rehabilitation clients?
> Secondary questions that I am asking are:
> What is the cost of post-rehabilitation addiction in both dollars and lives?
> Is there any information on the unhoused population of post-rehabilitation addicts-in-recovery that could help me design a community structure that might address those specific needs?
> What research methods have been used to investigate post-rehabilitation recovery communities?
> What are the methodological flaws in those methods?
> What connections might be made between maintaining sobriety and enacting life purpose?
> What problems have occurred for previous communities with similar goals?
> Definition of Terms
> 
> Addiction Recurrent behavior that accesses a substance or experience
> which produces pleasure or reduces discomfort but has negative social and functional consequences. Behavior repeats despite attempts to stop (Goodman, 1990).
> Intentional community Kozeny (1995) offers a helpful definition of intentional
> community: “An intentional community is a group of people who have chosen together with a common purpose, working cooperatively to create a lifestyle that reflects their shared core values. The people may live together on a piece of rural land, in a suburban home, or in an urban neighborhood, and they may share a single residence or live in a cluster of dwellings” (p.18).
> Life purpose “A central, self-organizing life aim that can be evaluated on
> the dimensions of strength (the influence it has on behavior), scope (the range of domains affected), and awareness (the degree to which there is conscious clarity and articulation)” (Kashdan et al., 2024).
> Meaningful work Michaelson (2019) defines meaningful
> work as both meaningful to the individual doing the work as well as to others.
> Recovery  Recovery is the experience (a process and a sustained status) through
> which individuals, families, and communities impacted by severe alcohol
> and other drug (AOD) problems utilize internal and external resources to voluntarily resolve these problems, heal the wounds inflicted by AOD-related problems, actively manage their continued vulnerability to such problems, and develop a healthy, productive, and meaningful life.” (White, 2007)
> Rehabilitation  The process by which a person addicted to a substance
> ​or process is brought to their maximal level of social and
> ​vocational functioning (Gogstad, 1987).
> Relapse  A return to routine, continued substance use after some period of
> ​sobriety (Gutierrez et al., 2020).
> Residential center  “A purposefully constructed, multi-dimensional living
> ​environment designed to enhance or provide treatment” (Whittaker
> ​et al., 2016, p. 94 in Daly et al., 2018).
> Substance use disorder (SUD)  The Diagnostic Statistical Manual of the
> American Psychiatric Association (2022) describes 11 different symptoms of SUD and then offers a scale of mild (exhibits two to three symptoms), moderate (exhibits four to five symptoms), and severe (exhibits six or more symptoms). The symptoms include: substance taken in larger amounts or over a longer period than was intended; there is a persistent desire or unsuccessful attempts to cut down or control use; a great deal of time is spent procuring, using or recovering from the effects of the substance; craving the substance; recurrent substance use resulting in a failure to fulfill role obligations at school, work, or home; continued substance use despite having interpersonal or social problems as a result of the use of the substance; important social, occupational, or recreational activities are given up because of the substance use; recurrent substance use in situations in which it is physically hazardous; substance use is continued despite knowledge of having physical or psychological problem that is likely to have been caused or exacerbated by use of the substance; tolerance, as defined by either a need for markedly increased substance in order to achieve the desired effect or markedly diminished effect with the use of the same amount of substance; withdrawal: symptoms of physical and/or psychological illness that is unique to each substance and that subsides either through taking the substance or through prolonged abstinence.
> Conclusion
> 
> ​Drug addiction is a massive societal problem which we have yet to solve. Many researchers have examined this problem and discovered various factors that help addicts-in-recovery to sustain their abstinence. Several established paths to sobriety have been fashioned that each have strengths and weaknesses. By learning from these existing knowledge bases and designing a community that combines the best practices among those factors that promote sobriety, we can provide an environment for addicts-in-recovery in which to deepen in their recovered state sufficiently to promote indefinite sobriety. Thus, Beyond Rehab could offer a model of sustainable recovery for unhoused addicts-in-recovery and those who wish to support them. Over the following chapters, I will review the literature on theories, interventions, and established methods that lead to sobriety. I will use ToC to carefully define the proposed community’s desired outcomes, denote its chosen interventions, and create a structure for measuring outcomes. I hope to generate a compelling narrative for not only how and why this community might be successful in achieving its aims, but also for why potential donors should fund it.

> Introduction
> My goal is to design a community that is sustained by its members and facilitates three main objectives: a solidification of recovery from addiction, the acquisition of portable job skills, and an opportunity to both explore and enact each participants life purpose. To that end, I am looking at the designs of other communities or programs with similar aims, as well as various factors that lead to sobriety. My interpretive lens in designing this community is transformative theory (Mertens 2008, 2011), and I am recruiting Breakwell’s (1983, 1986) and Deaux’s (1991, 1993) work on identity theory and various authors’ work on theory of change (ToC) to build a cohesive model of factors that lead to a shift of identity from addicted self to sovereign and sober self.
>             My primary question is: what social and financial best practices help communities promote sobriety in their members, support a search for meaning, and help members successfully reintegrate into society? Some secondary questions that I am asking are: what is the cost of post-rehab addiction in both dollars and lives? Is there any information on specific populations—particularly marginalized populations—of post-rehab addicts-in-recovery that could help me design a community structure that might address those specific needs? What research methods have been used to investigate post-rehab recovery communities? What are the methodological flaws in those methods? What connections might be made between maintaining sobriety and enacting life purpose? What problems have occurred for previous communities with similar goals?
> Theoretical Perspectives
> My interpretive lens in designing this community is transformative theory (Mertens 1999, 2008, 2011), which talks about the capacity for research to effect social change. Mertens’ background is as an evaluator of programs, and one of her main contentions is that clients need to be involved in the evaluation process (1999). This perspective will inform the design from intake to exit interviews. But, as Mertens points out, listening to the voices of our participants is not enough. “Transformation can only occur if this information is used to inform policies that effectively address the inequities that create the need for social programs” (Mertens, 1999, p. 12). In her 2011 work, Mertens emphasizes that designing programs so that the data produced will drive social change is key. As social change is an aspiration for this program, some of her recommendations have been incorporated into this work. These include looking for the resiliency in clients rather than for deficits and using direct empirical research along with scholarly textual investigation (Mertens, 1999). This project aims to take Mertens’ ideas on transformative theory and to make an effective, transformative praxis: a community that creates positive change.
> Theory of change
> To design and structure such a community, I use ToC, a body of work from the field of program evaluation. ToC provides a framework for evaluating whether any given intervention is causing the results it intends to cause (Funnell & Rogers, 2011). I took inspiration from experienced evaluators of public health initiatives (Mackenzie & Blamey, 2005) who state that a good ToC has plausability, do-ability, and testability. Thus, I selected evidence-based interventions that I thought I could (with a team of facilitators) carry out. I also built a variety of metrics into the design, so the community could generate data on the participants’ quality of life, and thus demonstrate whether or not the community will have had a positive effect.  Mackenzie and Blamey (2005) also write of a synergy between various inputs leading to a saturation point which can create powerful changes in participants. As initiating the oft-difficult transition from addiction to sovereignty is my goal for the participants, my design includes a panoply of interventions that I hope will synergize together to form such saturation and affect such change. To understand more deeply how to apply ToC, I read Funnel and Rogers’ seminal text from 2011, Purposeful Program Theory: Theories of Change and Logic Models. With their step-by-step guidance, I will develop a ToC for how I might plausibly help people to achieve sobriety and maintain it for at least 18 months.
> Identity theory 
> Breakwell’s Threatened Identities (1983) presents a comprehensive framework for understanding the construction, maintenance, and defense of identity in response to perceived threats. Grounded in social psychological theory, Breakwell conceptualizes identity as a dynamic and multifaceted construct governed by four fundamental principles: continuity, uniqueness (distinctiveness), self-esteem, and self-efficacy. Continuity refers to the persistence of identity across time, ensuring a sense of coherence despite developmental changes. Uniqueness underscores the individual's desire to maintain a distinct sense of self, differentiating oneself from others within social contexts. Self-esteem reflects the need for a positive self-concept, which is integral to psychological well-being, while self-efficacy pertains to the individual's belief in their capacity to influence outcomes and exercise control over their environment.
> ​The construction of identity is facilitated through two primary psychological mechanisms: assimilation-accommodation and evaluation (Piaget, 1952, Breakwell, 1983). Assimilation-accommodation describes the bidirectional process wherein new experiences are either integrated into pre-existing identity structures (assimilation) or necessitate a modification of identity to accommodate novel experiences (accommodation). Evaluation, on the other hand, involves the appraisal of one’s identity in relation to societal norms, values, and expectations, shaping self-perception and self-worth. However, identity is not a static entity; it is subject to external and internal forces that may pose threats to its integrity. These threats arise from various sources, including sociocultural transformations (e.g., migration, discrimination), personal crises (e.g., illness, loss), and conflicts with prevailing social norms (e.g., stigma, exclusion). When identity is threatened, individuals employ a range of coping mechanisms to mitigate psychological distress and restore equilibrium. These strategies include reevaluation, wherein the meaning of identity components is reinterpreted; compartmentalization, which involves maintaining distinct boundaries between conflicting identity aspects; and strategic social behavior, whereby individuals seek validation from social networks or avoid contexts that exacerbate the perceived threat.
> ​In her 1986 book, Coping with Threatened Identities, Breakwell describes how individuals cope when their identities are threatened (such as with addiction) through various means. One means is by joining a social group, and that process can help change the identity of the individual. Breakwell’s model remains highly influential in contemporary social psychology, providing a robust theoretical foundation for examining identity threat management, adaptation to social change, and the interplay between individual agency and societal structures. By elucidating the mechanisms through which identity is constructed, evaluated, and defended, her framework offers critical insights into the resilience of identity in the face of external pressures and internal conflicts. Understanding how identities are constructed and reconstructed is essential in helping guide clients from addiction to sobriety as the process involves the dissolution of an old identity and the reconstruction of a new one.
> Kay Deaux’s contributions to identity theory center on the understanding that identity is not merely an internal, self-directed construct but is shaped through dynamic transactions with social contexts, roles, and group memberships (Deaux, 1993; Deaux & Martin, 2003). Her framework—often situated at the intersection of social psychology and social constructionism—emphasizes identity as fluid, socially negotiated, and embedded in structures of meaning that are both cultural and relational. This relational turn in identity theory has profound implications for understanding the processes of addiction and recovery, domains in which identity is often unstable, stigmatized, and contested.
> Deaux (1993) conceptualized identity as composed of both personal identities (e.g., traits, autobiographical memories) and social identities (e.g., gender, race, occupation, group affiliations), asserting that the salience of these identities shifts according to context. In the case of addiction, social identity may become dominated by stigmatized labels such as “addict” or “junkie,” which are externally imposed and internalized through social feedback mechanisms. These labels often override more integrative or aspirational aspects of the self, reinforcing cycles of shame and social exclusion (McGaffin et al., 2013). Deaux's framework highlights how such identities are maintained and reinforced through repeated interactions with both institutions and peers, suggesting that recovery must involve not only behavioral change but also social reaffiliation and identity renegotiation.
> Moreover, Deaux's later work with Martin (2003) focused on the intersectionality and multiplicity of identity, arguing that individuals simultaneously navigate multiple identity positions which may conflict or cohere. For biracial women like Erika—who live at the intersections of race, gender, and socioeconomic precarity—Deaux’s model offers a way to understand how marginalization can become internalized within identity structures, contributing to emotional dysregulation and self-alienation. When addiction is introduced into this matrix, the sense of self may become even more fragmented, with substance use serving both as a coping mechanism and as an identity anchor in the absence of stable or affirming roles (Dingle et al., 2015).
> In recovery contexts, Deaux’s emphasis on social identity transformation is particularly salient. The process of joining recovery communities (e.g., 12-step programs, therapeutic groups) can be seen as adopting new social identities that are antithetical to those constructed during active addiction. These communities provide new role expectations, narrative scripts, and peer reinforcement, all of which contribute to identity reconstruction (Best et al., 2016). Deaux’s framework helps illuminate why mere abstinence may not suffice for sustainable recovery; without identity realignment and social reanchoring, individuals remain vulnerable to relapse due to unresolved identity dissonance.
> Thus, Deaux’s work bridges individual experience and sociocultural context in a way that is uniquely suited to address the complexities of addiction and recovery. Her contributions invite a shift from viewing recovery as an intrapsychic event to understanding it as a socially mediated process of reidentification, in which individuals reconstruct self-understandings within new social matrices of meaning, belonging, and purpose.
> Theories of Addiction
> 
> ​Even the definition of addiction has varied widely over time, let alone its etiology. Originally, the latin words ad dicere meant to speak or pronounce, as in a sentence (Simpson et al., 1989). Indeed, an addict in Roman contract law was someone, usually a debtor, who had been sentenced either to prison or slavery, and so addictus came to mean ’to be bound to another person’ (Lemon, 2018). This is quite resonant with our modern usage of addiction, as many addicts feel enslaved to their substance of choice (Valverde, 1997). In 16th century England, the usage of addiction expanded, and it was common to speak of being addicted to hobbies or even going to church (Lemon, 2018), and addiction came to mean an extreme devotion to God. This could also be seen as fitting, as many addicts make their substances of choice into gods. While there have been a few definitions of addiction over time, there are a veritable multitude of theories on the root of addiction.
> ​The study of addiction etiology has evolved considerably over time, reflecting shifts in scientific paradigms, cultural attitudes, and methodological approaches (Heather & Robertson, 1983). Theories of addiction range from moralistic interpretations to complex biopsychosocial models (West, 2006). Over the centuries, numerous theories have been proposed, each offering a different perspective on the underlying causes of addiction, ranging from spiritual and moral explanations to biological and sociocultural frameworks (White, 2000). Scholars estimate that well over a hundred theories on addiction have emerged throughout history, reflecting the dynamic and evolving nature of the field (Shaffer et al., 1997; White, 2000). This literature review categorizes these theories into six broad categories: moral models, psychological models, biological models, sociocultural models, indigenous models, and trauma-based models (White, 2000). Each category represents a significant historical and theoretical development in understanding addiction.
> Indigenous Models
> Indigenous perspectives on trauma and addiction often conceptualize substance use not simply as an individual pathology, but as a disruption of spiritual balance, communal harmony, and cultural identity (Gone, 2013). Rather than isolating the phenomenon within biomedical frameworks, many Indigenous traditions interpret addiction as a visible manifestation of deeper wounds caused by colonization, historical oppression, forced assimilation, and the intergenerational transmission of collective suffering (Brave Heart, 1998; Rowan et al., 2015). The concept of “historical trauma” has been widely used to describe these cumulative psychological and spiritual injuries experienced across generations, leading to heightened vulnerability to addiction and mental distress (Sotero, 2006).
> Healing approaches in Indigenous communities emphasize the restoration of balance through cultural reconnection, collective responsibility, and spiritual practices rather than reliance on exclusively clinical interventions (Kirmayer, Gone, & Moses, 2014). Sweat lodges provide an opportunity to purify the body, mind and spirit and connect with fellow community members through song and prayer (Marsh, Marsh, Ozawagosh, & Ozawagosh, 2015; Wall, 2007). Storytelling helps addicts-in-recovery digest their experience of addiction by creating a narrative framework that facilitates meaning-making and identity reconstruction (Rowan et al., 2014; Wendt, 2016). Rites of passage such as vision quests allow participants to call in preferred identities and embody them, all the while supported by community to step into their new selves (Lertzman, 2002; Riley, 2008; Wood, 2010). Traditional healing strategies may also include ceremonies such as the sundance, storytelling, and land-based activities, which not only address substance use but also reaffirm cultural continuity and identity (Brady, 1995; Rowan et al., 2014; Fiedeldey-Van Dijk, 2017). These practices underscore the relational worldview in Indigenous epistemologies, which positions healing as a communal endeavor embedded in land, ancestors, and spiritual forces (Walters, Simoni, & Evans-Campbell, 2002).
> Indigenous models of healing align with holistic frameworks that integrate emotional, physical, mental, and spiritual dimensions of health, resisting the reductionist tendencies of dominant biomedical models (Dell & Hopkins, 2011). By incorporating sociocultural and trauma-based understandings of addiction, these approaches provide a distinct, culturally grounded pathway to recovery that affirms resilience, cultural revitalization, and the sovereignty of Indigenous knowledge systems (Hartmann & Gone, 2016).
> Moral Models
> ​The earliest theories of addiction, dating back to antiquity and persisting through the 19th century, were rooted in moral interpretations (Levine, 1978). Addiction was often seen as a failure of willpower or a sinful indulgence (Berridge, 1978). Religious perspectives, such as those found in early Christian doctrine, framed addiction as a moral failing requiring spiritual intervention (Levine, 1978). The temperance movement of the 19th and early 20th centuries further reinforced this view, advocating for abstinence and moral reform as solutions to substance abuse (White, 1998).
> Psychological Models
> ​With the advent of modern psychology in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, addiction began to be understood through various psychological frameworks (Shaffer, 1997). Sigmund Freud’s psychoanalytic theory suggested that substance use might be an attempt to manage unconscious conflicts (Freud, 1897). In the same paper, Freud puts forth masturbation as our earliest addiction, predating the mid-1980s introduction of sexual addiction to psychology by nearly a century (Schwartz & Brasted, 1985). Carl Jung proposed that addiction could be a misguided attempt at achieving individuation, where substances temporarily replace a sense of spiritual wholeness (Jung, 1961). Heinz Kohut’s self-psychology model argued that addiction stems from early deficits in self-cohesion, leading individuals to seek substances as a means of compensating for unfulfilled developmental needs (Kohut, 1971). Otto Kernberg extended this view, suggesting that personality disorders and object relations dysfunction play a role in substance dependence (Kernberg, 1975). Later, behavioral psychology introduced the concept of addiction as a learned behavior, influenced by conditioning and reinforcement (Skinner, 1963). Cognitive-behavioral approaches emerged in the latter half of the 20th century, emphasizing the role of cognitive distortions and maladaptive thought patterns in SUDs (Beck et al., 1993).
> Biological Models
> ​Biological theories of addiction gained prominence in the mid-20th century with advances in neuroscience and pharmacology (Leshner, 1997). Early medical models suggested a genetic predisposition to addiction (Jellinek, 1960), while neurobiological research identified changes in brain chemistry, particularly in the dopamine system, as a critical factor in substance dependence (Koob & Le Moal, 1997). The disease model, championed by the American Medical Association and Alcoholics Anonymous, is close to the received view in cognitive neuroscience. Melis and colleagues (2005) sum up the view well:
> [N]ot different from traditional diseases, drug addiction bears with it a number of biological abnormalities, which justifies the label disease. Although repetitive use of drugs affects different organs, the primary target appears to be the brain – thus brain disease (p. 102).
>  
> One benefit of the medical model is that it conceptualizes addiction as a chronic, relapsing brain disease in which repeated substance use produces lasting neuroadaptations across reward, stress, and executive control systems rather than a personal failure (Koob & Volkow, 2016). According to this view, addiction emerges from a shift in the brain’s hedonic “set point”—a process called allostasis—where normal levels of reward become insufficient and stress circuitry becomes over-activated (Koob, 2013).
> At the binge/intoxication stage, substances produce exaggerated dopaminergic and opioid activity in the mesolimbic system, reinforcing drug-taking behavior (Volkow et al., 2019). With chronic use, however, the brain reduces its sensitivity to dopamine, leaving the individual in a hypodopaminergic state when not using (Volkow et al., 2019). In the withdrawal/negative affect stage, stress-related systems such as corticotropin-releasing factor (CRF) and norepinephrine (NE) are recruited, creating dysphoria, irritability, and anxiety, which strongly motivate relapse (Koob & Volkow, 2016). Finally, in the preoccupation/anticipation stage, impairments in prefrontal cortical circuits weaken executive control and heighten craving, making the cycle of relapse difficult to break (Koob, 2013). This tripartite model has extensive empirical support from neuroimaging, animal models, and clinical research, and is considered the dominant integrative biological theory of addiction in academic and medical communities (Koob & Volkow, 2016; Volkow et al., 2019).
> NE, a neurotransmitter central to arousal, stress, and attention, has been increasingly recognized as a critical modulator of addiction beyond the classic dopaminergic framework (Weinshenker & Schroeder, 2007). The noradrenergic system, arising primarily from the locus coeruleus, plays a key role in the regulation of stress responses and withdrawal symptoms, linking it directly to relapse vulnerability (Sofuoglu, 2009). During withdrawal from substances such as opioids, stimulants, and alcohol, hyperactivation of NE pathways contributes to symptoms including anxiety, irritability, and dysphoria, which drive compulsive drug-seeking as a form of negative reinforcement (Aston-Jones & Cohen, 2005). Pharmacological evidence further supports NE’s role in addiction: agents that modulate noradrenergic activity, such as alpha-2 adrenergic agonists (e.g., clonidine, lofexidine), have been shown to reduce withdrawal severity and craving in both clinical and preclinical studies (Sofuoglu, 2009).
> While dopamine has dominated addiction neuroscience, the integration of NE highlights addiction as a disorder involving the dynamic interplay between reward and stress circuits (Weinshenker & Schroeder, 2007). In Koob and Volkow’s allostatic model, NE is positioned within the extended amygdala as a central player in the recruitment of anti-reward and stress systems that perpetuate compulsive drug use (Koob & Volkow, 2016). The recognition of NE’s importance has not only expanded neurobiological theories of addiction but also informed therapeutic development, positioning noradrenergic modulation as a promising adjunct in the treatment of SUDs (Sofuoglu, 2009).
> A second influential biological theory of addiction is Reward Deficiency Syndrome (RDS), first posited by Kenneth Blum and colleagues 30 years ago, and developed consistently since then (Blum et al., 1995, 2018). RDS posits that genetic and environmental factors converge to produce hypodopaminergic functioning within the mesolimbic reward pathway, which results in diminished sensitivity to natural rewards such as social connection, achievement, and pleasure (Blum et al., 2022a). In this model, individuals with deficient dopamine signaling may engage in substance use or maladaptive behaviors in an attempt to artificially stimulate dopaminergic activity and restore hedonic homeostasis (Blum et al., 2022b).
> The framework of RDS has gained traction because it integrates neurogenetics, epigenetics, and psychosocial factors into a unified explanation of vulnerability to addiction and related disorders (Blum et al., 2018). In the latest iteration of Blum and colleagues’ thoughts on RDS, they argue that it is not merely a modern disorder but an evolutionary phenomenon rooted in human biology and behavior (Blum et al., 2022c). They contend that genetic variants influencing dopaminergic pathways, such as DRD2 polymorphisms, historically promoted adaptive traits like impulsivity and risk-taking that aided survival but now predispose individuals to addictive and compulsive behaviors (Blum et al., 2022c). Extending beyond SUDs, RDS is framed as a universal condition underlying maladaptive behaviors including overeating, gambling, and excessive internet use, making it, in their words, “blowin’ in the wind” across cultures and populations (Blum et al., 2022c). This universalizing perspective also serves to destigmatize maladaptive behavior by locating it in shared biological vulnerabilities rather than individual moral failings (Blum et al., 2022c). By situating RDS in an evolutionary context, the authors emphasize the need for personalized, genetically informed treatments that integrate both biological predispositions and environmental influences in managing addiction (Blum et al., 2022c). Moreover, it extends beyond substance use to encompass behaviors such as pathological gambling, overeating, and compulsive internet use, suggesting that these seemingly disparate conditions may share a common dopaminergic deficit at their core (Blum et al., 2022a). Contemporary research in precision addiction management further refines RDS by proposing genetic testing and dopamine-enhancing interventions as individualized treatments for those identified with reward-processing deficits (Blum et al., 2022b).
> A third influential biological model of addiction is the Incentive-Sensitization Theory (IST) developed by Robinson and Berridge, which proposes that the core pathology of addiction lies not in the hedonic or pleasurable effects of drugs, but in the sensitization of mesolimbic dopamine pathways that amplify the incentive salience—or “wanting”—associated with drug cues (Robinson & Berridge, 2008). Unlike tolerance, which reduces hedonic pleasure over time, sensitization causes drug-related stimuli to become hyper-salient, producing intense craving even in the absence of heightened enjoyment (Robinson & Berridge, 2016). According to this model, repeated exposure to addictive substances induces long-lasting neuroadaptations in the mesocorticolimbic dopamine system, which cause pathological levels of motivation toward drug-seeking while dissociating “wanting” from “liking” (Robinson & Berridge, 2008).
> A major strength of IST is its ability to explain why relapse can occur after prolonged abstinence: sensitized incentive-salience circuits can remain dormant but become reactivated by drug cues, stress, or priming doses years after cessation (Robinson & Berridge, 2016). This framework is supported by extensive animal research, where repeated drug exposure increases dopamine reactivity to cues, as well as by human neuroimaging studies showing heightened striatal responses to drug-associated stimuli in addicted populations (Volkow et al., 2019). Within the addiction research community, IST is regarded as one of the most influential and widely accepted psychological-neurobiological models of addiction, often integrated with complementary frameworks such as the allostatic model and reward-deficiency perspectives to create multidimensional accounts of addictive processes (Robinson & Berridge, 2016; Volkow et al., 2019). IST, RDS, and the allostatic models are only the most widely accepted frameworks within the medical community that help to explain addiction. There are many other models (see Greener & Storr, 2023 for a review), and we can no longer speak of ‘a’ medical model, but rather a panoply of models that may conflict or work together to help explain behavior and offer potential avenues for therapeutic interventions.
> Sociocultural Models
> ​Sociocultural perspectives on addiction consider the broader environmental, social, and cultural influences that shape substance use behaviors (Room, 2003). Classic sociological theories, such as those of Émile Durkheim and Robert Merton, linked addiction to societal structures, stress, and anomie (Durkheim, 1897; Merton, 1938). More contemporary models incorporate concepts such as social learning (Bandura, 1977), the impact of socioeconomic status, and the role of cultural norms in shaping substance use patterns (Rhodes, 2002). Some scholars argue for a public health approach that incorporates harm reduction strategies, recognizing that the abstinence model is not for everyone (Marlatt, 1996).
> Trauma-Based Models
> Gabor Maté’s work on addiction has introduced a trauma-based perspective that emphasizes the role of early childhood adversity, emotional pain, and psychological stress in the development of addiction (Maté, 2008). Maté argues that addiction is not simply a biological disease but a response to unresolved trauma and a means of coping with deep-seated emotional wounds. His model integrates elements of neuroscience, psychology, and social context to explain how early experiences shape brain development and increase vulnerability to addiction (Maté, 2010).
> Other trauma-based models have been developed by researchers such as Bessel van der Kolk, who highlights the role of post-traumatic stress and body memory in addiction (van der Kolk, 2014). Judith Herman’s work on complex trauma and its links to substance use emphasizes how prolonged exposure to traumatic stress shapes self-perception and coping mechanisms (Herman, 1992). Bruce Perry’s neurodevelopmental model underscores how early life trauma alters brain development, increasing susceptibility to addiction (Perry & Szalavitz, 2006). These perspectives align with broader research on adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) and their long-term impact on mental health and substance use (Felitti et al., 1998). Trauma-informed treatment approaches based on these models emphasize compassion, self-awareness, and healing rather than punishment or abstinence-focused interventions (Maté, 2018; van der Kolk, 2014; Perry & Szalavitz, 2006).
> Factors that aid in recovery from addiction
> The literature suggests that there are many factors that lead to successful and sustained recovery from addiction. These include motivation, time, love, 12-step programs and supportive environments that encourage a sense of connection, family, social consequences, hope for recovery, peer group change, meaning in life, developing a favorable sense of self and envisioning a positive future (Koski-Jännes & Turner, 1999, Dekkers et al., 2021, Leamy et al., 2011, Du Plessis, 2010, and Speigelman, 2015). Snoek (2016) also noted the importance of developing concrete strategies and the creation of the proper environment to help prevent relapse rather than relying on willpower. Banks (2021), after doing a phenomenological study of six addicts who had sustained recovery for up to 30 years, determined that the sole commonality among them was a pursuit of PTSD treatment along with their various treatment modalities for SUD.
> Motivation
> For Miller (1999), motivation is the key factor that drives recovery from addiction, and he thus makes Motivational Interviewing (MI) a central technique in his recovery program. In a randomized clinical trial of 60 women in Iran undergoing substance abuse treatment, MI was found to increase motivation to cease using drugs by about 81% (Oveisi et al., 2020). Santa Ana and colleagues (2021) conducted a randomized control that found that the use of group MI upon entrance to a rehabilitation program resulted in increased attendance at 12-step meetings and decreased number of alcohol use days, binge drinking days, and overall amount of drinking. Miller et al.(2003), however, found no appreciable difference in outcomes when adding MI to standard drug treatment in a randomized trial of 208 randomized patients, so there is some controversy on whether this technique can help clients.

> Social support is a complex phenomenon that has both tangible and intangible aspects (Sheldon & McKay, 2020). Person (1986) counts five separate scales that measure social support and argues that social support networks help clients cope with challenging circumstances. This finding was bolstered by Mpanza and colleagues (2022), who spoke to a wide range of social service providers in Kwa-Zulu Natal who provided aftercare for clients returning home to their families. In that context, the service providers identified recreational outlets, vocational opportunities, and the importance of repairing broken familial bonds as causative factors helping their clients stay sober. Luciano and colleagues (2014) found that social support is particularly helpful for those addicts-in-recovery who also have a co-occurring mental health diagnosis. They added that maintaining a regular schedule and having a healthy mindset were also common factors in sobriety of the 12 subjects in their qualitative study. Hall (2019) concurs with much of the previous research on the importance of social support to sobriety and adds that the more trust that exists within a social circle, the greater its bolstering effects on sobriety. Zaidi (2020) adds that social media and other digital types of social support can help addicts-in-recovery find a sense of purpose and reduce feelings of social isolation.

> Mindfulness
> Since Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) was created in the early 2000s, adapting the ancient practice of mindfulness to a modern, medical context (Kabat-Zinn, 2003), many practitioners have developed their own versions targeted specifically at Substance Use Disorders (SUDs). For example, programs such as Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention (MBRP) (Witkiewitz et al., 2005) and Mindfulness-Oriented Recovery Enhancement (MORE) (Garland, 2018) were tailored to target the mechanisms believed to underlie addiction. There is significant statistical evidence to show that these methods help reduce cravings in addicts-in-recovery (Garland & Howard, 2018; Korecki et al., 2020). Li and colleagues (2017) performed a meta-analysis and found that mindfulness-based interventions (MBIs) were more effective than control conditions (e.g., treatment as usual [TAU], relapse prevention treatment [RP], cognitive behavioral therapy [CBT], support group) at reducing the frequency and amount of substance use, the number of substance-related problems, and the level of craving, while increasing rates of abstinence. Moreover, when Korecki and colleagues (2020) endeavored to run another meta-analysis, they started to classify these mindfulness practices into those that they posited would be more helpful early in the recovery process and those that would be more helpful later. They also noted that one protocol had been formulated to target a specific substance (Mindfulness training for smoking cessation, created by Brewer et al., 2011). Rosenthal and colleagues (2021) have also noted that numerous meta-analyses demonstrated the clinical efficacy of MBIs in the treatment of addictive disorders. The above evidence argues that a deep and flexible program of mindfulness that could be tailored for individual participants would be most efficacious.
> ​Theoretical Mechanisms and Neurobiological Changes. Garland and Howard (2018) theorize five principal mechanisms underlying the reconfiguration of reward processing in addiction: (i) the enhancement of executive functioning; (ii) the augmentation of dispositional mindfulness; (iii) the attenuation of stress reactivity; (iv) the mitigation of cue reactivity; and (v) the reduction of thought suppression. These findings align with evidence from a randomized controlled trial (RCT) investigating cognitive domains such as working memory, selective attention, and decision-making among polysubstance users, wherein the implementation of mindfulness meditation yielded statistically significant improvements in these capacities (Valls-Serrano et al., 2016). Moreover, RCTs utilizing MBRP and MORE have demonstrated that increases in dispositional mindfulness—particularly in dimensions such as acceptance, present-moment awareness, and nonjudgment—serve as significant mediators in the observed reductions in craving post-intervention (Garland et al., 2016; Witkiewitz et al., 2013).
> MBI-Induced Neurobiological Adaptations.
> Mindfulness practice induces neuroplastic changes in brain regions critical for self-regulation and reward processing, thereby directly targeting the neural dysfunctions seen in SUDs. Specifically, mindfulness has been shown to strengthen executive functioning and cognitive control (Hölzel et al., 2011; Garland & Howard, 2018), restore natural reward processing (Garland et al., 2025), and train practitioners to generate blissful states endogenously, which replace their exogenous cravings for pleasure through substances.
> Hölzel and colleagues (2011) found that MBIs increase gray matter density in brain areas responsible for executive function, such as the Prefrontal Cortex (PFC) and the Anterior Cingulate Cortex (ACC). This neuroplastic change enhances top-down control, improving the individual’s capacity to regulate attention, manage urges, and override automatic, habitual responses to substance cues (Garland & Howard, 2018). Garland and colleagues created
> the MORE protocol and included processes that specifically target the anhedonia (blunted capacity for pleasure) that drives substance craving. Studies show that MORE training helps restore the brain’s response to natural, healthy rewards, evidenced by an increase in functional connectivity between prefrontal control regions and subcortical reward centers like the ventral striatum (Garland et al., 2025). Some of Garland and colleagues earlier (2022) research found
> that mindfulness practice both reduced craving in opiate users and induced states of non-dual awareness that were often accompanied by the affective quality of bliss. Furthermore, this research identified that the experience of self-transcendence during meditation was correlated with an increase in frontal midline theta EEG power, suggesting a specific neurophysiological mechanism by which the pursuit of a drug-induced high is replaced by a naturally available, mindful state (Garland et al., 2022).
> Bishop and colleagues (2004), Choi and colleagues (2021), and Zinn (2003) all write about the connection between mindfulness and acceptance, as the activation of acceptance seems to be part of the therapeutic action of mindfulness. However, not all mindfulness practices lead to greater levels of acceptance, and so explicitly naming the goal of acceptance when introducing the practice of mindfulness seems to be important.

> Comprehensive MBI Approaches and Comorbidity,
> The efficacy of MBIs is further supported by the growing evidence for third-wave behavioral approaches that heavily utilize mindfulness, such as Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT). ACT is an empirically supported treatment that focuses on increasing psychological flexibility—the ability to fully contact the present moment and persist or change behavior in the service of chosen values (Hayes et al., 2013).
> Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT).
> While MBRP and MORE explicitly integrate mindfulness for relapse prevention, ACT offers a broader, transdiagnostic framework. Meta-analyses demonstrate that ACT is a promising intervention, showing equivalent efficacy to established treatments like CBT for SUDs, with some studies suggesting ACT may yield superior abstinence rates immediately following treatment (Lee et al., 2015; Ruano et al., 2024). ACT's core component of cognitive defusion—learning to observe thoughts (e.g., "I need a drink") as mental events rather than literal commands—directly supports the mechanism of reducing thought suppression, which is essential in navigating urges without resorting to use.
> Addressing Heterogeneity and Dual Diagnosis.
> Sancho and colleagues (2018) did a systematic review of 54 RCTs involving various MBIs and found that the most effective intervention was a combination of MBI and treatment as usual (TAU). This speaks to the complexity of addiction and the importance of a multitude of simultaneous modalities. One study the Sancho group cited (Negrei et al., 2015) found that MBI + CBT resulted in lower scores in depression and anxiety than a comparative group taking psychiatric medication.
> Furthermore, MBIs are proving vital in treating the high rates of comorbidity between SUDs and other mental health conditions (e.g., anxiety and depression). Research suggests that mindfulness and acceptance strategies are particularly effective for individuals with dual diagnoses because they directly target the common underlying deficit of emotion dysregulation (Amaro & Black, 2021). For example, a study of adults with stimulant dependence found that mindfulness training was especially beneficial for those with co-occurring depression or anxiety, helping them manage psychological discomfort without turning to drugs (Glasner et al., 2016). The ability of MBIs to modulate negative emotional states and attenuate stress reactivity thus makes them a powerful, flexible tool across various substance and patient profiles.
> ​Methodological Concerns in MBI Research.
> Despite the growing body of supportive evidence, the field of MBI research for SUDs faces persistent methodological limitations. Demina and colleagues (2023) found that the majority of RCTs involving MBIs were of low quality and had a high risk of bias due to issues that were hard to overcome. For instance, many studies rated high on risk of bias in measuring their outcomes because participants knew which intervention they received (lack of blinding). The high rate of attrition in some studies led them to measure high on risk of bias for deviating from the intended intervention. This points to the need to design future studies that might overcome these obstacles, potentially through more robust, active control groups that are equally engaging and resource-intensive, or by focusing on objective biomarkers rather than subjective reports.

> Shift in Social Identity
> Several authors have written on the importance of shifting one’s social identity in the process of moving from addiction to sobriety (Best et al., 2016, 2018, Buckingham et al., 2013, Dingle et al., 2015, Johansen et al., 2013, Kellogg, 1993). The less one identifies as an addict and the more closely one’s locus of identity is centered in a state of recovery, the less one’s chance for relapse. Best et al. (2017) demonstrated that this trend extends to 12-step practitioners, who generally identify themselves to be “addicts in recovery” rather than “recovered”. Best and colleagues (2017) showed that those who identify as still “in recovery” have a higher rate of relapse than those who identify themselves as “recovered.”
> Kellogg (1993) does a good job of synthesizing previous research on identity and elucidates a coherent process by which addicts move from an identification with themselves as addicted to a state of identity in which they are free from their addiction. He finds that there are three paths that addicts typically take towards the creation of a new identity: identity reversion, identity extension and identity emergence. Identity reversion occurs when there are enough positive elements of the addict’s pre-addiction life that survived the addiction and that the addict can use to re-create a sober identity. Identity extension refer to the refocusing of one’s energy on an identity that co-existed with the addict identity and which was either untouched by or not so badly damaged by the addiction that it could not be salvaged. Identity emergence is necessary when the addict has no access to a social world that could be used as a suitable vehicle to create a sober identity. In such cases, a new social milieu is required to provide social validation and social acceptance of the new identity by non-addicts in order to 1) remove the individual from old behavior patterns and the temptations associated with addict friends; 2) help redefine drug use in a harmful context; and 3) it provides alternative activities and behaviors (Biernacki, 1986; Waldorf, 1983).
> Meaningful Work
> Bailey and colleagues (2019) performed the important work of tracing the development of the concept of meaningful work, analyzing how 71 groups of researchers have defined the term over time, yielding six dominant perspectives, of which four are salient for the purposes of a long-term center for recovery from addiction.
> The first perspective defines meaningful work as a psychological state which results from an optimal level of skill variety, task significance, and task identity. A subgroup within this research uses Spreitzer’s (1995) work on psychological empowerment, which he defines as a multifaceted construct containing “a set of four cognitions reflecting an individual’s orientation to his or her work role: meaning, competence… self-determination, and impact” (p. 1443). Keeping this definition in mind, Spreitzer (1995) then defines meaningfulness as “the value of a work goal or purpose, judged in relation to an individual’s own ideals or standards” (p. 1443). Spreitzer’s work has been used by May and colleagues (2004) and others (e.g., Montani et al., 2017; Soane et al., 2013) to develop scales of meaningful work and study role engagement in various contexts.
> The second perspective identified by Bailey and colleagues (2019) comes from the theoretical work of Ashmos and Duchon (2000), who stress the employee’s inner life and the community context of work to derive meaning. Ashmos and Duchon (2000) name their approach “workplace spirituality” and posit that organizations can facilitate human flourishing by cultivating an environment that fulfills individuals’ intrinsic spiritual needs, including inner life enrichment, engagement in meaningful work, and a sense of communal belonging. For Ashmos and Duchon (2000), meaningful work creates a sense of joy that connects workers to a greater good and to things which the worker values in life. Ashmos and Duchon (2000) also developed a widely used scale (e.g., Arnold et al., 2007; Pradhan & Pradhan, 2016) called The Meaning and Purpose at Work Scale.
> The third perspective on meaningful work found by Bailey and colleagues (2019) comes from the humanities tradition. Scholars within this field define meaningful work as having both objective features such as enablement of autonomy, freedom, and social recognition, along with offering workers the subjective experience of meaningfulness (Wolf, 2010; Yeoman, 2014). Researchers within this domain have suggested that the deepest sense of meaning arises from coherence across four qualities: unity with others, expressing oneself, serving others, and developing/becoming oneself (e.g., Lips-Wiersma & Morris, 2009; Lips-Wiersma et al., 2016). Another way that meaningful work is conceived within the humanities is as “fulfilling, significant, directed, coherent with life goals, and contributing to a sense of belonging” (Schnell et al., 2013, p. 548).
> The fourth perspective on meaningful work identified by Bailey and colleagues (2019) is prevalent in three scholarly divisions (positive psychology, spirituality, and humanities), and denotes meaningfulness as a “multifaceted eudaimonic psychological state” (p. 91). One group of scholars within this stream argues that meaningful work has three facets: the deeply personal resonance of fulfillment that individuals extract from their labor, the intricate interplay between vocational meaning and the broader tapestry of one's existence, and the innate aspiration to leave a benevolent imprint upon the world, forging a legacy of purpose and contribution (Bergmann et al., 2014; Rasmussen et al., 2016; Steger et al., 2012). Another group of scholars within this stream (e.g., Chen et al., 2016; Hirschi, 2012) use the theoretical work of Bunderson and Thompson (2009), who define meaningfulness in work as any activity which the participant views as a calling. Their scale has two core facets: work that is important to the individual and work that makes a difference to the world (Bunderson & Thompson, 2009).
> On a more practical level, Bell and colleagues (2024) discovered the surprising fact that greater health in post-rehabilitation addicts-in-recovery was correlated with employment but was not correlated with the amount of the wages earned. This speaks to the intrinsic value of meaningful work outside of the amount of financial remuneration and suggests that meaningful works plays a role far more significant than the raising of one’s status in capitalism’s pecking order.

> The Eudaimonic Imperative in Addiction Recovery.

The shift from substance dependence to sustained sobriety is not merely the cessation of substance use (a hedonic outcome); it is fundamentally a process of eudaimonic recovery—the cultivation of meaning, purpose, and self-realization (Garland, 2018). Within the context of positive psychology, meaningful work is considered a significant driver of this eudaimonic well-being and is central to theories of human flourishing (Seligman, 2012; Steger & Dik, 2009). The concept of meaningful work, as a subjective experience that one's work is particularly significant, aligns with one's core values, and contributes to some greater good or higher purpose, provides an anchor for individuals in recovery, expanding self-worth and acting as a powerful buffer against relapse (Rosso et al., 2010).
>  
> Addiction is often characterized by a shift from the pursuit of natural rewards and life goals to a narrow focus on the immediate, intense pleasure (hedonia) of the substance. This process leads to a profound eudaimonic deficit, characterized by a loss of purpose, disconnection from values, and impaired functioning in key life areas such as work, family, and community (Garland, 2018). The literature indicates that individuals with alcohol dependence, for example, show a significant deficiency in measures of eudaimonia and mindfulness compared to non-dependent individuals (Joshua George, et al., 2023). This deficit suggests that for recovery to be truly sustainable, treatment must focus on the proactive enhancement of well-being, rather than solely the alleviation of negative symptoms. Relapse risk may remain high if the primary reason for hedonic indulgence—eudaimonic deficiency—is left unaddressed (Joshua George, et al., 2023).
> Mechanisms of Meaningful Work in Recovery.

> First, securing meaningful work or engaging in job readiness programs provides financial stability and a sense of achievement, instilling confidence and replacing the chaotic lifestyle of active addiction with a structured, purposeful routine (Walton & Hall, 2016). The structured nature of work reduces idle time, which is a common trigger for relapse (Lundgren & Chassman, 2023).

> Music Therapy
> Music therapy has emerged in recent years as a promising adjunctive intervention for people with substance use disorders, with systematic reviews concluding that adding music therapy to standard care likely reduces craving and can increase motivation for treatment and change (Ghetti et al., 2022). Music-based approaches used in addiction treatment include receptive listening, songwriting, group improvisation, lyric analysis, and structured music-making aimed at emotion regulation, social bonding, and skill-building (Pasqualitto et al., 2023). Randomized and quasi-experimental trials and feasibility studies have reported moderate reductions in craving and improvements in emotional regulation when music therapy is delivered alongside detoxification or short-term rehabilitation services, with stronger effects when therapy comprises more than a single session (Fachner et al., 2023). Narrative and experimental work also point toward music therapy’s capacity to scaffold motivation, increase treatment engagement, and provide non-verbal avenues for processing trauma-related material that commonly co-occurs with substance misuse (Pasqualitto et al., 2023).
> Contemporary neurobiological and process-focused studies propose specific mechanisms by which music therapy may ameliorate addiction-related processes, including modulation of reward circuitry, attenuation of conditioned craving responses, and enhancement of top-down emotion regulation through repeated, emotionally salient, yet safe music experiences (Pasqualitto et al., 2023). Pilot work using neurophysiological measures such as EEG and fMRI alongside psychometrics is beginning to map how music-evoked affect and therapist-guided musical interaction can alter neural signatures linked to craving and memory reconsolidation for addiction-related cues (Fachner et al., 2023). Process studies further suggest that songwriting and improvisation give clients concrete ways to externalize inner states, rehearse adaptive coping narratives, and co-create alternative identities to the “addict” role—mechanisms theorized to reduce relapse risk via changes in self-concept and social belonging (Pasqualitto et al., 2023).
> Although the music-and-addiction literature has concentrated largely on inpatient and outpatient clinical samples, a smaller but growing body of research has examined music therapy with people who are unhoused or living in shelters, and this work indicates particular promise for community-based, low-barrier music interventions with this population (Williams, 2013). Qualitative studies and program evaluations with homeless adults report that open group music workshops, choir initiatives, and drop-in music therapy sessions can increase feelings of social connection, reduce loneliness, provide meaningful structured time, and offer non-stigmatizing spaces for emotional expression—outcomes that are tightly linked to reduced substance-use risk and improved psychosocial functioning (Rio, 2005; Williams, 2013). Case descriptions of community programs, such as music-based peer initiatives for veterans and community music therapy projects, similarly suggest music’s capacity to re-engage people in recovery capital–building activities such as employment training, group performance, and peer leadership—pathways especially relevant for people exiting homelessness (Rio, 2005; Williams, 2013).
> Despite these encouraging findings, research with unhoused groups vary widely in design, sample size, and outcome measurement, leaving important gaps about for whom and under which conditions music therapy is most effective in homelessness contexts (Sample, 2019). Methodological limitations reported across studies include small convenience samples, variability in intervention dose and fidelity, a reliance on qualitative outcomes, and infrequent longitudinal follow-up, which together constrain causal inference and generalizability (Ghetti et al., 2022; Williams, 2013). Consequently, best-practice recommendations emphasize integrating music therapy into low-threshold, trauma-informed, culturally responsive service hubs; combining music therapy with concrete supports such as housing navigation, employment services, and primary care; and designing mixed-methods, longitudinal trials with objective craving and relapse indices as well as psychosocial measures to test mechanisms and real-world effectiveness for people who are unhoused (Fachner et al., 2023; Pasqualitto et al., 2023; Williams, 2013).

> Life Purpose
> Victor Frankl’s (1985) theory posited that a lack of existential meaning—what he termed an “existential vacuum”—can lead individuals to fill the void with maladaptive behaviors, including substance use. He observed that individuals who lacked a sense of purpose or direction were more vulnerable to nihilism, apathy, and escapist behaviors (Frankl, 1985).
> In contemporary research, this existential model has inspired investigations into how the presence or pursuit of meaning in life (MIL) may serve as a psychological buffer against addiction (Park, 2010). Yet the empirical landscape remains mixed, with some studies confirming protective associations while others highlight important moderating variables or null findings (Sliedrecht et al., 2020; Csabonyi & Phillips, 2020).
>  
> Csabonyi and Phillips (2020) provided evidence supporting Frankl’s original hypothesis, showing that higher presence of meaning was significantly associated with reduced drug and alcohol use in a sample of young adults (Csabonyi & Phillips, 2020). > Psilocybin
> In recent years, psilocybin-assisted therapy has garnered growing interest as a novel intervention for SUDs. Psilocybin, a classic psychedelic compound found in certain species of mushrooms, has demonstrated potential in disrupting entrenched behavioral patterns associated with addiction (Bogenschutz & Johnson, 2016). Its mechanism appears to involve temporary destabilization of rigid neural networks, allowing for the introduction of new perspectives and behavioral flexibility (Carhart-Harris & Friston, 2019). This process may be particularly beneficial for individuals with addiction, a disorder often marked by cognitive and behavioral rigidity (Kalivas & Volkow, 2005).
> Early clinical studies have shown promising results, particularly in the treatment of tobacco and alcohol use disorders. In a pilot study conducted at Johns Hopkins University, 80% of participants who received psilocybin-assisted therapy for smoking cessation were abstinent at six months, compared to 35% in traditional behavioral interventions (Johnson et al., 2014). Similarly, a randomized controlled trial by Bogenschutz et al. (2022) demonstrated significant reductions in heavy drinking days among participants with alcohol use disorder, with effects persisting at 32 weeks post-intervention.
> The therapeutic efficacy of psilocybin appears closely tied to the quality of the acute subjective experience it induces, particularly those involving mystical-type experiences characterized by a sense of unity, transcendence, and emotional catharsis (Griffiths et al., 2006). These experiences often lead to enhanced insight, reduced craving, and increased motivation to change (Garcia-Romeu et al., 2015). Neuroimaging studies suggest that psilocybin decreases activity in the default mode network (DMN), a system involved in self-referential thinking, rumination, and craving—all of which play central roles in addictive pathology (Carhart-Harris et al., 2012).
> Importantly, psilocybin is not administered as a stand-alone drug treatment but is embedded within a psychotherapeutic context that includes preparation, guided sessions, and integration therapy (Ross et al., 2016). This model distinguishes it from conventional pharmacotherapies and aligns it more closely with existential and psychospiritual approaches to recovery, which aim not merely to reduce symptoms but to facilitate personal transformation (Noorani et al., 2018).
> Despite encouraging outcomes, there remain limitations. Most studies to date are small-scale, with limited diversity in sample populations, and often lack long-term follow-up data (Reiff et al., 2020). Additionally, the risks associated with unsupervised psychedelic use underscore the need for controlled environments and trained facilitators (Johnson et al., 2008).
> Nevertheless, psilocybin represents a paradigm shift in the treatment of addiction—one that emphasizes psychological insight, meaning-making, and neurobiological plasticity. Further large-scale, multisite trials are underway and are likely to clarify the role of psilocybin in mainstream addiction medicine.
> Ayahuasca
> Ayahuasca, a traditional Amazonian plant brew containing the psychoactive compound N,N-dimethyltryptamine (DMT) along with monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs) such as harmine, has emerged as a potential therapeutic agent in the treatment of SUDs (Labate & Cavnar, 2014). Though historically used in indigenous rituals, ayahuasca has more recently been incorporated into structured therapeutic and ceremonial contexts aimed at psychological healing and self-inquiry (Fábregas et al., 2010). Unlike conventional treatments, ayahuasca ceremonies often emphasize experiential insight, emotional catharsis, and reconnection with self and others, elements increasingly recognized as central to recovery (Loizaga-Velder & Verres, 2014).
> Several observational and qualitative studies suggest that ayahuasca use may lead to sustained reductions in substance use, particularly for individuals with histories of polysubstance dependence (Thomas et al., 2013). A study conducted in a Canadian detox center found that participation in ayahuasca-assisted therapy led to significant improvements in measures of depression, anxiety, and empowerment, all of which are linked to relapse risk (Nielson et al., 2018). Participants also reported a reduced urge to use substances and enhanced clarity regarding the root causes of their addiction (Talin & Sanabria, 2017).
> Ayahuasca’s pharmacological action—primarily its activation of the serotonin 5-HT2A receptor—may foster temporary neuroplasticity, enhancing cognitive flexibility and emotional processing (Riba et al., 2006). This mechanism is especially relevant for addiction, which is increasingly understood as a disorder of maladaptive predictive coding and rigid behavioral patterns (Koob & Volkow, 2016). Neuroimaging studies have demonstrated that ayahuasca modulates activity in the default mode network, reducing rumination and promoting insight (Palhano-Fontes et al., 2015). These neural shifts mirror those observed with other psychedelics, though the presence of MAOIs and the prolonged duration of the ayahuasca experience make it unique in therapeutic application.
> In addition to neurochemical effects, the therapeutic outcomes of ayahuasca appear deeply influenced by the context in which it is consumed—often referred to as "set and setting" (Tupper, 2008). The ceremonial framework, typically led by experienced facilitators, may enhance feelings of safety, trust, and interpersonal connection, which are themselves predictive of better recovery outcomes (Loizaga-Velder & Verres, 2014). Furthermore, users frequently report experiences of spiritual awakening, re-evaluation of past trauma, and enhanced motivation for behavioral change (Bouso et al., 2012).
> However, methodological limitations abound in current research. Most studies are observational, lack randomization, and rely on self-report data, limiting causal inference (Reckweg et al., 2022). Despite this, the consistent themes across studies—improved psychological insight, reduction in craving, and increased resilience—warrant further investigation into ayahuasca as a complementary or alternative modality in addiction treatment.
> Psychotherapy
> The effectiveness of psychotherapy for SUDs has been established across a variety of modalities, though the mechanisms by which therapeutic change occurs remain a subject of ongoing debate. Multiple treatment approaches—including cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), psychodynamic therapy, motivational interviewing (MI), and contingency management—have demonstrated efficacy in the treatment of addiction, with meta-analyses often revealing modest but consistent effect sizes (Dutra et al., 2008; Magill & Ray, 2009). Importantly, the literature does not consistently support the superiority of one theoretical orientation over others. Rather, many modalities produce comparable outcomes, suggesting that factors shared across treatments may play a more substantial role in therapeutic effectiveness than modality-specific techniques (Wampold & Imel, 2015).
> This apparent equivalency has led researchers to examine the so-called "common factors" in psychotherapy. These include elements such as the therapeutic alliance, therapist empathy, client expectancy, and the provision of a rationale for treatment (Norcross & Lambert, 2018). Among these, the therapeutic alliance has emerged as one of the most robust predictors of outcome across diagnostic categories and treatment types, including those for substance use (Flückiger et al., 2018). Even within highly structured or manualized interventions such as CBT, the quality of the therapist-client relationship consistently predicts engagement, retention, and symptom reduction (Meier et al., 2005).
> In the treatment of addiction specifically, patient-level variables appear particularly salient. Motivation to change, psychological mindedness, relational capacity, and severity of co-occurring disorders often shape the course and outcome of therapy (Project MATCH Research Group, 1997). For example, motivational interviewing—a modality developed specifically for ambivalent clients—has shown superior outcomes among individuals with low initial readiness to change (Miller & Rollnick, 2013). Conversely, individuals with greater psychological insight and reflective capacity may benefit from psychodynamic or integrative therapies that address unconscious conflicts and relational patterns (Leichsenring & Steinert, 2017).
> Beyond specific modalities or techniques, client factors have repeatedly been found to account for the largest proportion of variance in psychotherapy outcomes. In a landmark analysis, Asay and Lambert (1999) estimated that client variables—such as readiness for change, interpersonal skills, and life circumstances—account for approximately 40% of outcome variance, compared to only 15% attributed to specific techniques. This holds true for substance use populations as well, where comorbid psychopathology, trauma histories, and socioeconomic instability often complicate treatment trajectories (Najavits, 2002).
> Nevertheless, some therapy-specific effects have been observed in certain contexts. For instance, contingency management—an operant conditioning-based approach that provides tangible rewards for abstinence—has demonstrated strong efficacy in promoting short-term behavioral change among stimulant users (Higgins et al., 1994; Petry et al., 2005). Similarly, cognitive-behavioral interventions that target coping skills and cognitive distortions have been effective in reducing relapse rates (Carroll & Onken, 2005). These findings suggest that while common factors provide a foundation for therapeutic change, targeted interventions may offer additional benefits when aligned with client needs and contexts.
> In sum, psychotherapy is demonstrably effective in the treatment of addiction, but its mechanisms of action appear to lie more in the relational and contextual features of the therapeutic process than in the exclusive use of any particular technique. A pluralistic and client-centered model that integrates evidence-based modalities with attention to therapist-client dynamics, patient readiness, and environmental supports offers the most promising path forward in addiction treatment.
> Hope
> Hope has long been recognized as a central psychological resource in overcoming adversity, including addiction (Snyder, 2002). Defined within positive psychology as the perceived capacity to find pathways toward goals and the motivation to use those pathways, hope incorporates both agency (willpower) and pathways (planning) (Snyder et al., 1991). This dual-component model has been widely applied to the study of recovery from SUDs, where lack of hope is frequently associated with relapse, treatment dropout, and despair (Irving et al., 2004). Conversely, higher hope has been shown to predict greater treatment engagement, improved abstinence rates, and enhanced psychological well-being (Mathis et al., 2009).
> Hope is often diminished in individuals struggling with addiction, especially when they have experienced repeated failures in quitting, social isolation, or comorbid depression (Gonzalez et al., 2006). Therapeutic interventions that seek to restore or amplify hope can thus serve as a foundation for initiating and maintaining change (Cheavens et al., 2006). In a longitudinal study of individuals in residential treatment for substance use, higher baseline levels of hope were associated with better retention and lower post-treatment substance use (Berg et al., 2011). Moreover, hope has been shown to mediate the relationship between self-efficacy and abstinence, suggesting it plays a crucial motivational role in sustaining behavior change (Green et al., 2009).
> One reason hope may be such a strong protective factor is that it counteracts the fatalism and learned helplessness that often accompany chronic addiction (Snyder, 2002).
> Clients who believe that change is possible are more likely to engage in goal setting, take constructive action, and cope with setbacks adaptively (Cheavens et al., 2006).
> Narrative studies have shown that people in recovery often describe “turning points” or moments of awakening that reignited their hope and reoriented their life stories (Gibson, 2013). This reconnection to hope can mark the beginning of an identity shift from “addict” to “survivor” or “rebuilder” (McAdams, 2001).
> Therapeutic models have increasingly incorporated hope-based principles into addiction treatment (Irving et al., 2004). Motivational Interviewing, for example, is designed to elicit intrinsic motivation and optimism about change, fostering hope through empathic dialogue and goal alignment (Miller & Rollnick, 2012). Likewise, Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) cultivates psychological flexibility and value-guided action, offering clients a framework for pursuing meaningful goals even in the face of relapse or craving (Hayes et al., 2011). These approaches emphasize that hope is not simply a trait but a skill that can be developed and practiced (Snyder, 2002).
> Group-based recovery communities such as Alcoholics Anonymous (AA) and SMART Recovery also explicitly center hope as a driving force in recovery (Vaillant, 2005). Testimonies of long-term sobriety, peer support, and shared vision reinforce the belief that a better future is achievable, especially when modeled by others with similar struggles (Krentzman, 2013). In these settings, hope becomes both relational and communal, shaped by belonging and mutual encouragement (Best et al., 2016). This intersubjective hope can sustain recovery even in moments when individual motivation falters (Best et al., 2016).
> Nevertheless, hope must be distinguished from unrealistic optimism, which can undermine recovery by minimizing the severity of addiction or overestimating one’s control (McKay & Tokar, 2012). Effective treatment cultivates realistic hope—grounded in both the acknowledgment of difficulty and the belief in one’s capacity for progress (Snyder et al., 1991).
> Cognitive distortions, depression, and trauma can obstruct hope, and addressing these barriers is essential to its restoration (Gonzalez et al., 2006). Thus, hope-building may require both intrapsychic interventions (e.g., cognitive restructuring) and relational supports (e.g., mentoring, community) (Irving et al., 2004).
> In sum, hope is a dynamic, multifaceted construct that plays a vital role in addiction recovery across cognitive, emotional, behavioral, and social domains (Snyder, 2002). By promoting goal-directed thinking and emotional resilience, hope helps individuals envision and enact a life beyond substance dependence (Mathis et al., 2009). Integrating hope-enhancement strategies into addiction treatment may not only improve outcomes but also restore a sense of dignity and possibility for those on the path to healing (Gibson, 2013).

> Alcoholics Anonymous (AA)
> AA was founded in 1935 in Akron, Ohio, by Bill Wilson, a New York stockbroker, and Dr. Bob Smith, an Akron surgeon, both of whom were themselves alcoholics (Alcoholics Anonymous World Services, 2001). Its emergence was influenced by the Oxford Group, a Christian fellowship that emphasized spiritual renewal through confession and moral inventory (Kurtz, 1991). Wilson and Smith adapted these ideas into a more inclusive, non-denominational framework, articulating them in Alcoholics Anonymous (1939), commonly referred to as "The Big Book." Central to AA’s method is the notion that alcoholism is a chronic disease with physiological, psychological, and spiritual dimensions. The solution, according to AA, is total abstinence supported by sustained participation in group meetings and the working of the 12 Steps, which are designed to facilitate personal transformation, emotional healing, and spiritual awakening.
> The Twelve Steps. The 12 Steps constitute the core of AA’s recovery methodology. Each step corresponds to a stage of personal development and communal accountability. The following descriptions of each step reflect the original language and intent as outlined in The Big Book, with commentary based on scholarly interpretations.
> 1. “We admitted we were powerless over alcohol—that our lives had become unmanageable” (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, p. 21). This step reflects the foundation of AA: acceptance of addiction as a loss of control. White (2004) discusses how this admission begins a process of surrender and self-honesty that can reduce denial and defensiveness. Research emphasizes the therapeutic value of admitting powerlessness, which aligns with the transtheoretical model's concept of readiness for change (DiClemente, 2003). This invocation of powerlessness is paradoxical in AA because while AA’s members humbly profess their lack of power in the face of addiction, they demonstrate a powerful capacity to abstain from alcohol with the support of community and their respective higher powers.
> 2. “We came to believe that a Power greater than ourselves could restore us to sanity” (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, p. 44).  Tonnigan and colleagues (2002) found that belief in a higher power—even among non-religious individuals—was associated with greater treatment engagement and abstinence, indicating the step's role in fostering hope and external orientation. However, this central focus on spirituality in AA that is rooted in Judeo-Christian sources has drawn some criticism that it excludes members who from atheistic, agnostic or non Judeo-Christian spiritual traditions (Trice & Staudenmeier, 1989)
> 3. “Made a decision to turn our will and our lives over to the care of God as we understood Him” (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, pp. 60-61).  The language of surrender here functions psychologically as a method of reducing ego defenses and fostering humility, a trait correlated with long-term recovery (Vaillant, 2003).
> 4. “Made a searching and fearless moral inventory of ourselves” (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, p. 64). Often compared to cognitive-behavioral therapy's use of self-monitoring, this step encourages reflective awareness and accountability (Galanter, 2007). Vaillant (2005) considers this step a turning point, encouraging emotional catharsis and insight that enable deeper psychological healing and behavior change.
> 5. “Admitted to God, to ourselves, and to another human being the exact nature of our wrongs” (Alcoholics Anonymous, 2001, p. 72). Confession is framed here as relational and cathartic. Studies suggest that the act of disclosure, particularly in trusted contexts, enhances emotional regulation and social bonding (Kelly, 2017).  Flores (2004) views this step through the lens of attachment theory, noting that relational confession can reduce shame and foster intimacy, which is essential for emotional regulation.
> 6. "Were entirely ready to have God remove all these defects of character" (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, pp. 76).  Kurtz (2002) interprets this as a spiritual readiness that underscores the shift from self-centeredness to humility, preparing individuals for ongoing transformation. Kaskutas (2009) similarly sees this step as encouraging a posture of willingness and non-defensiveness, which may increase receptivity to behavioral change.
> 7. "Humbly asked Him to remove our shortcomings" (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, p. 76).  Miller & Kurtz (1994) explain that this step reinforces spiritual surrender and encourages the development of moral and emotional maturity.
> 8. "Made a list of all persons we had harmed, and became willing to make amends to them all" (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, pp. 76-77).  Morgenstern et al. (1996) suggest this step increases motivation for change by engaging cognitive dissonance and personal accountability. The reparative ethic in this step echoes principles of restorative justice and contributes to improved interpersonal functioning (Doweiko, 2012).
> 9. "Made direct amends to such people wherever possible, except when to do so would injure them or others" (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, pp. 78-79).  Zemore et al. (2004) demonstrate that making amends not only helps repair social bonds but also provides therapeutic benefit to the person making amends, often reducing relapse risk. Kelly & White (2012) found that behavioral restitution, when safe, is associated with decreased guilt and increased self-efficacy in recovery populations.
> 10. "Continued to take personal inventory and when we were wrong promptly admitted it" (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, p. 84). Moos & Moos (2006) link this step to long-term recovery maintenance, noting that ongoing self-monitoring is associated with sustained sobriety and improved coping.
> 11. "Sought through prayer and meditation to improve our conscious contact with God as we understood Him" (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, pp. 85-86). Robinson et al. (2011) found that increased spiritual practices, including meditation, correlated with reduced alcohol use and enhanced psychological well-being over time. Zemore (2007) discovered that mindfulness and prayer are linked with relapse prevention by increasing psychological flexibility and emotional resilience.
> 12. "Having had a spiritual awakening as the result of these steps, we tried to carry this message to alcoholics, and to practice these principles in all our affairs" (Alcoholic Anonymous, 2001, p. 89).  Pagano et al. (2013) provide empirical evidence that helping others through service activities enhances self-efficacy and reinforces abstinence, echoing the "helper therapy" principle. White (2007) adds that service to others and integration of values are seen as protective factors against relapse and markers of sustained identity transformation

> Efficacy and Outcome Research. AA’s effectiveness has been studied extensively, though often with methodological complexity due to its decentralized structure and ethical constraints on randomized trials. Project MATCH, one of the most influential studies on treatment matching, found that individuals who attended AA frequently had better drinking outcomes, particularly among those with higher levels of alcohol dependence (Project MATCH Research Group, 1997). Longitudinal data from Vaillant (2003, 2012) and others have shown that consistent participation in AA (defined as weekly or more frequent meeting attendance) is positively correlated with sustained abstinence, sometimes even outperforming professional treatment modalities. Kelly et al. (2020), in a comprehensive Cochrane review, concluded that AA and Twelve Step Facilitation (TSF) interventions produce comparable or superior outcomes to cognitive-behavioral therapy in promoting long-term abstinence.
> Nevertheless, rates of sustained sobriety vary widely. A review by Kaskutas (2009) suggests that only about 20–25% of AA newcomers remain continuously abstinent after one year, though those who stay for 90 days or more show higher success rates. Factors influencing retention include social support, spirituality, and congruence with personal values.
> Critiques of the AA Model. Despite its wide reach, AA has faced substantial critique from academic, medical, and advocacy communities. Chief among these is the religious or spiritual framing of recovery, which some view as exclusionary or ineffective for secular individuals (Humphreys, 2004). While AA emphasizes that “God” may be interpreted broadly, critics argue that theistic assumptions are embedded in its core language and structure. Others have challenged the emphasis on powerlessness, arguing that this stance may undermine self-efficacy and reinforce helplessness (Peele, 2001).
> Another critique centers on the lack of formal accountability or clinical oversight. AA’s peer-led structure, while empowering in many ways, raises concerns about the potential spread of misinformation, variable group quality, and the lack of evidence-based psychiatric support for co-occurring disorders (Room & Greenfield, 2013). Feminist and trauma-informed critics have also highlighted that the moral inventory and confession aspects may retraumatize individuals with histories of abuse (Kasl, 1992). Despite these critiques, AA offers an excellent example of  the application of several of the factors that lead to recovery discussed in the previous section. AA uses social support, spiritual awakening, and shift in social identity to help addicts make the leap from addiction to sobriety. Other organizations offer a different mix of factors.
> SMART Recovery
> SMART Recovery (Self-Management and Recovery Training) is a non-profit, evidence-informed addiction recovery program grounded in cognitive-behavioral and motivational enhancement principles (Horvath & Yeterian, 2012; SMART Recovery, 2023). Developed in 1994 as a secular alternative to the 12-step model popularized by Alcoholics Anonymous (AA), SMART Recovery is based on the belief that individuals struggling with addiction can achieve abstinence through the cultivation of self-reliance, emotional regulation, and rational decision-making (Brooks & Penn, 2003; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). Unlike AA, which posits addiction as a lifelong disease necessitating spiritual surrender, SMART Recovery treats addiction as a maladaptive behavior that can be changed through structured interventions and personal empowerment (Kelly et al., 2020; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012).
> Theoretical Foundations and Core Principles. SMART Recovery is primarily rooted in principles derived from cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT), and Motivational Interviewing (MI) (Beck et al., 1993; Ellis, 1962; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). These frameworks collectively emphasize the role of distorted thinking, emotional dysregulation, and ambivalence in the maintenance of addictive behaviors (Beck et al., 1993; Miller & Rollnick, 2012). REBT, developed by Albert Ellis, is particularly influential in SMART Recovery’s emphasis on identifying and disputing irrational beliefs that perpetuate substance use (Ellis, 1962; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). In contrast to deterministic views of addiction, SMART Recovery promotes the notion that individuals possess agency and can change their behavior through conscious effort and psychological restructuring (Beck et al., 1993; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012).
> At the heart of the SMART Recovery model lies the 4-Point Program, which serves as a flexible framework rather than a rigid sequence (SMART Recovery, 2023; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). The four points include: (1) Building and Maintaining Motivation, (2) Coping with Urges, (3) Managing Thoughts, Feelings, and Behaviors, and (4) Living a Balanced Life (SMART Recovery, 2023). These points guide participants through a process of self-exploration, cognitive reframing, and lifestyle redesign (Horvath & Yeterian, 2012; Brooks & Penn, 2003). Importantly, SMART Recovery does not adopt the disease model of addiction but views addictive behaviors as learned and reinforced patterns that can be unlearned and replaced (Beck et al., 1993; SMART Recovery, 2023).
> Program Structure and Tools. SMART Recovery meetings, facilitated by trained volunteers or professionals, are designed to be interactive and participatory (SMART Recovery, 2023; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). Participants are encouraged to share their experiences, engage in collaborative problem-solving, and practice self-monitoring (Brooks & Penn, 2003; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). A typical meeting may involve reviewing tools such as cost-benefit analysis (CBA), ABC (Activating event, Beliefs, Consequences) worksheets, or the Hierarchy of Values exercise—each aimed at enhancing insight and promoting behavioral change (Ellis, 1962; Beck et al., 1993; SMART Recovery, 2023).
> One of the model’s signature tools is the ABC analysis derived from REBT, which helps participants identify the irrational beliefs that mediate between a triggering event and maladaptive behavior (Ellis, 1962; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). For example, a person who relapses after an argument may, through ABC analysis, uncover the belief “I can’t stand being criticized,” and work toward replacing it with a more adaptive thought such as “I don’t like criticism, but I can handle it” (Ellis, 1962; Beck et al., 1993). Such cognitive restructuring is central to the SMART philosophy, which holds that emotional resilience and behavioral control are outcomes of more rational thinking (Beck et al., 1993; SMART Recovery, 2023).
> Additionally, the CBA tool encourages individuals to weigh the short-term and long-term consequences of using versus not using substances (Beck et al., 1993; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). This helps to destabilize the romanticized view of substance use and reinforce more reality-based motivations for abstinence (Bogenschutz et al., 2020; SMART Recovery, 2023). The emphasis on skill acquisition and self-efficacy aligns SMART Recovery closely with contemporary cognitive-behavioral treatment modalities (Beck et al., 1993; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012).
> Unlike 12-step programs, which place a strong emphasis on surrendering to a higher power and accepting powerlessness, SMART Recovery encourages individuals to develop a sense of personal control and responsibility (Brooks & Penn, 2003; Kelly et al., 2020). While spirituality is not excluded, it is not framed as a necessary component for recovery (SMART Recovery, 2023; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). This makes the model especially appealing to individuals who are secular, skeptical of religious doctrine, or disillusioned with traditional recovery paradigms (Zemore et al., 2018; Brooks & Penn, 2003).
> Empirical Support and Comparative Effectiveness
> Although SMART Recovery is not as extensively studied as 12-step programs, a growing body of literature supports its efficacy (Kelly et al., 2020; Zemore et al., 2018). In a comparative study by Zemore et al. (2018), individuals who participated in alternative mutual-help groups like SMART Recovery reported comparable improvements in abstinence, psychological well-being, and quality of life to those attending AA (Zemore et al., 2018). Importantly, these benefits were more pronounced among individuals who preferred secular recovery models or who expressed discomfort with the spiritual components of 12-step programs (Zemore et al., 2018; Brooks & Penn, 2003).
> Another randomized controlled trial by Bogenschutz et al. (2020) found that participation in SMART Recovery was associated with increased self-efficacy, improved emotional regulation, and reduced substance use over a 6-month follow-up period (Bogenschutz et al., 2020). These findings support the theoretical premise that enhancing cognitive and emotional skills can yield clinically meaningful outcomes (Beck et al., 1993; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). Furthermore, the flexible, self-directed nature of SMART Recovery aligns with contemporary understandings of recovery as a non-linear and individualized process (Laudet & White, 2010; Bogenschutz et al., 2020).
> However, it is important to note that SMART Recovery may not be effective for all individuals (Kelly et al., 2020; Brooks & Penn, 2003). Some participants may struggle with the cognitive demands of the program, particularly those with co-occurring mental health disorders, cognitive impairments, or lower levels of education (Laudet & White, 2010; Kelly et al., 2020). Moreover, the absence of a strong community or spiritual framework may render the program less effective for individuals who benefit from the communal and existential support found in 12-step groups (Kelly et al., 2020; Zemore et al., 2018). Thus, clinical practitioners must carefully assess client preferences, values, and cognitive capacities when recommending a recovery model (Bogenschutz et al., 2020; Laudet & White, 2010).
> Clinical and Philosophical Implications. SMART Recovery’s emphasis on autonomy, rationality, and self-determination introduces important philosophical and clinical questions about the nature of addiction and the path to recovery (Ellis, 1962; Beck et al., 1993). By rejecting the disease model and affirming the possibility of recovery without spiritual surrender, SMART Recovery challenges dominant narratives in the addiction treatment landscape (Horvath & Yeterian, 2012; SMART Recovery, 2023). It re-centers recovery on the development of agency, psychological insight, and adaptive behavior (Beck et al., 1993; Brooks & Penn, 2003). As such, SMART utilizes shift in identity, psychotherapy and social support as its main tools in helping addicts-in-recovery to deepen in their sovereignty. This last factor, social support, is not as strong in SMART Recovery as it is in AA.
> Recovery Dharma
> Recovery Dharma is a peer-led, non-theistic addiction recovery community that utilizes Buddhist principles and meditation practices as the foundation for healing from substance use and compulsive behaviors (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021). The model emerged in 2019 from a restructuring of Refuge Recovery, itself an earlier Buddhist-based recovery approach founded in 2014 by Noah Levine (Levine, 2014; Recovery Dharma, 2020). Following a leadership crisis in Refuge Recovery, the community reorganized democratically to emphasize decentralized, inclusive governance, giving rise to what is now known as Recovery Dharma (Larkin et al., 2021; Recovery Dharma, 2020). This model offers a distinctive alternative to conventional programs like Alcoholics Anonymous by grounding recovery not in surrender to a higher power, but in mindfulness, ethical living, and self-investigation (Monteiro, Musten, & Compson, 2015; Kelly & Greene, 2018).
> Theoretical foundations. Recovery Dharma is based on the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path of Buddhism, which are adapted to address the specific suffering associated with addiction (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021). The First Truth identifies the presence of suffering, which in the context of addiction includes craving, shame, and disconnection (Levine, 2014; Marlatt, 2002). The Second Truth emphasizes that suffering is caused by craving and attachment, including the compulsive use of substances or behaviors to avoid pain or pursue pleasure (Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999; Recovery Dharma, 2020). The Third Truth asserts that freedom from suffering is possible, offering hope and self-efficacy to individuals who have felt powerless in their addiction (Monteiro et al., 2015; Kelly & Greene, 2018). The Fourth Truth, the path to liberation, is actualized through the Eightfold Path, which becomes a roadmap for ethical, mindful living and recovery (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021).
> The Eightfold Path in Recovery Dharma consists of right understanding, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, and concentration, all of which are interpreted through the lens of trauma-informed care and non-theistic spirituality (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Monteiro et al., 2015). Right understanding involves recognizing addiction as a form of suffering and cultivating insight into its causes, including trauma and systemic oppression (Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999; Larkin et al., 2021). Right intention fosters the development of compassion and non-harming toward self and others, counteracting the shame and self-blame common in addiction (Levine, 2014; Kelly & Greene, 2018). Right speech and action encourage honesty, accountability, and non-harming behavior, which are essential for rebuilding trust in relationships (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Monteiro et al., 2015). Right livelihood emphasizes ethical work and reducing exploitation, connecting recovery with broader social values (Marlatt, 2002; Recovery Dharma, 2020).
> Methods. Right effort, mindfulness, and concentration are particularly emphasized through the regular practice of meditation, which is a cornerstone of the Recovery Dharma model (Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999; Larkin et al., 2021). Members are encouraged to engage in daily mindfulness meditation to observe cravings, emotions, and thoughts without reacting to them impulsively (Monteiro et al., 2015; Recovery Dharma, 2020). This emphasis on direct experience and self-awareness distinguishes Recovery Dharma from more externally directed approaches to recovery, such as the 12-step model (Kelly & Greene, 2018; Levine, 2014). The cultivation of present-moment awareness helps participants develop greater emotional regulation and distress tolerance—skills that are predictive of sustained abstinence (Witkiewitz, Marlatt, & Walker, 2005; Larkin et al., 2021).
> A central practice in Recovery Dharma is inquiry, which involves a deep, structured examination of past behaviors, motivations, and relationships using Buddhist psychological frameworks (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Monteiro et al., 2015). Participants undertake a process of personal inventory, akin to Step Four in the 12-step model, but without invoking a higher power or confession to others (Kelly & Greene, 2018; Levine, 2014). Instead, the emphasis is on self-compassionate awareness and letting go of fixed identities, particularly the addict identity that can perpetuate shame and limit growth (Larkin et al., 2021; Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999). Inquiry is typically supported by peer-led groups, which provide relational accountability without hierarchical sponsorship structures (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Monteiro et al., 2015).
> Recovery Dharma meetings are peer-facilitated, secular, and consensus-based, reflecting the Buddhist value of sangha, or spiritual community (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021). Meetings often include meditation, readings from the Recovery Dharma book, shared reflections, and compassionate listening, creating a container that supports both personal and collective healing (Monteiro et al., 2015; Marlatt, 2002). The model emphasizes inclusivity and accessibility, with explicit commitments to anti-racism, gender inclusion, and trauma awareness (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021). These commitments reflect an understanding that addiction is both a personal and social condition, shaped by interlocking systems of oppression, marginalization, and trauma (Witkiewitz et al., 2005; Marlatt, 2002).
> The empirical base for Buddhist-informed recovery is still emerging, but promising evidence supports the efficacy of mindfulness-based approaches for SUDs (Witkiewitz et al., 2014; Garland et al., 2010). Mindfulness-based relapse prevention (MBRP), which shares significant overlap with Recovery Dharma, has been shown to reduce substance use, improve emotional regulation, and decrease relapse risk in multiple randomized controlled trials (Witkiewitz et al., 2014; Bowen et al., 2009). These outcomes support the core assertion of Recovery Dharma that mindfulness and non-reactivity are protective factors against addiction (Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999; Garland et al., 2010). Moreover, the incorporation of ethical living and community support may enhance engagement and retention compared to purely individualistic interventions (Monteiro et al., 2015; Larkin et al., 2021).
> Critiques of Recovery Dharma. Critics of Recovery Dharma and similar models caution that the program’s cognitive and introspective demands may not be suitable for all individuals, particularly those with severe psychiatric comorbidities or cognitive impairments (Kelly & Greene, 2018; Witkiewitz et al., 2005). The lack of a centralized structure or formal treatment professionals may also be a barrier for those who prefer clinician-led interventions (Larkin et al., 2021; Marlatt, 2002). However, its accessibility, emphasis on agency, and holistic view of recovery make Recovery Dharma a powerful alternative for individuals disillusioned with traditional or theistic models (Monteiro et al., 2015; Levine, 2014).
> Summary. Philosophically, Recovery Dharma reframes addiction not as a disease or a moral failing, but as a natural human response to suffering and disconnection (Marlatt, 2002; Recovery Dharma, 2020). This view reduces stigma and opens space for compassion, both toward oneself and others in the recovery process (Monteiro et al., 2015; Larkin et al., 2021). By emphasizing impermanence, interdependence, and self-inquiry, Recovery Dharma encourages individuals to move beyond rigid identities and cultivate freedom from conditioned patterns (Levine, 2014; Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999). As such, it represents not only a recovery program, but a comprehensive spiritual and psychological path that continues to grow in relevance as recovery culture becomes increasingly pluralistic (Kelly & Greene, 2018; Witkiewitz et al., 2014). > Delancey Street
> The Delancey Street Foundation is one of the most well-known and long-standing residential self-help organizations for individuals recovering from substance use, criminal behavior, and poverty (Mitchell et al., 2001; Schlosser, 2005). Founded in 1971 in San Francisco by Mimi Silbert and John Maher, Delancey Street operates as a structured, peer-run therapeutic community (TC) grounded in principles of mutual responsibility, social enterprise, and long-term behavioral transformation (Silbert & Pines, 1983; De Leon, 2000). Unlike conventional addiction treatment programs that rely on clinical professionals, Delancey Street is entirely staffed and operated by its residents, who enter voluntarily and commit to staying a minimum of two years without external funding or medication-assisted treatment (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Mitchell et al., 2001). The model emphasizes personal accountability, meaningful work, community immersion, and a shift in social identity as the primary agents of change (Schlosser, 2005; De Leon, 2000).
> Methods. The program is based on the therapeutic community (TC) model, which conceptualizes addiction not as an isolated disease, but as a symptom of broader deficits in social functioning, identity formation, and self-regulation (De Leon, 2000; Magura, 2007). Delancey Street applies this framework by creating a total environment in which every element—from chores to job training—is viewed as part of the therapeutic process (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Mitchell et al., 2001). Central to this model is the notion that the “community as method” approach can re-socialize individuals who have been marginalized or criminalized due to their addiction and behavior (De Leon, 2000; Borkman et al., 1998). This approach relies heavily on peer feedback, role modeling, and experiential learning rather than formal psychotherapy or pharmacological treatment (Magura, 2007; Schlosser, 2005).
> Delancey Street differs significantly from other recovery models in its emphasis on self-governance and mutual teaching (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Mitchell et al., 2001). New residents are paired with senior peers who mentor them through daily responsibilities, life skills, and behavioral expectations (De Leon, 2000; Borkman et al., 1998). These mentor-mentee relationships are seen as essential for breaking entrenched patterns of dependency and instilling values such as discipline, integrity, and delayed gratification (Schlosser, 2005; Silbert & Pines, 1983). The program does not allow for drug replacement therapies or psychiatric medications, instead prioritizing immersive behavioral restructuring through communal living and labor (Mitchell et al., 2001; Magura, 2007).
> Residents at Delancey Street engage in full-time participation in social enterprises run by the organization, including moving companies, restaurants, catering services, and construction businesses (Schlosser, 2005; Silbert & Pines, 1983). These businesses serve dual purposes: generating revenue for the organization and providing residents with real-world vocational training and work habits (De Leon, 2000; Mitchell et al., 2001). Each resident progresses through a meritocratic system in which they take on increasing levels of responsibility, both in business operations and in community governance (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Borkman et al., 1998). This structure reinforces the development of self-esteem, economic competence, and leadership capacity—core dimensions of the program’s definition of recovery (De Leon, 2000; Magura, 2007).
> Theoretical model. The theoretical underpinnings of Delancey Street are influenced by behavioral psychology, humanistic education, and social learning theory (De Leon, 2000; Borkman et al., 1998). The community acts as a corrective emotional experience in which maladaptive behaviors are openly confronted through group feedback and modeled alternatives (Mitchell et al., 2001; Magura, 2007). Negative behaviors, such as manipulation, dishonesty, or entitlement, are addressed through peer-led interventions and accountability structures, not punitive measures (Schlosser, 2005; Silbert & Pines, 1983). Residents are expected to “act as if” they are already responsible, capable individuals—a concept drawn from cognitive-behavioral and existential traditions that suggest identity can be reshaped through disciplined performance (De Leon, 2000; Borkman et al., 1998).
> Unlike 12-step programs or clinical treatment models, Delancey Street refrains from labeling its participants as addicts or alcoholics (Schlosser, 2005; Silbert & Pines, 1983). The focus is not on pathology but on potential: every resident is treated as a student capable of change and excellence (Mitchell et al., 2001; Borkman et al., 1998). This strengths-based approach fosters dignity, agency, and intrinsic motivation, which are critical for sustaining long-term behavioral change (Magura, 2007; De Leon, 2000). By providing opportunities for leadership, education, and entrepreneurship, the program reframes recovery as a process of social reintegration and self-actualization (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Schlosser, 2005).
> Empirical research. Empirical research on Delancey Street is limited but promising, with several qualitative and retrospective studies documenting low recidivism rates, sustained abstinence, and vocational reintegration among graduates (Mitchell et al., 2001; Borkman et al., 1998). A study by Mitchell and colleagues (2001) found that among criminal justice-involved individuals, participation in Delancey Street was associated with significantly lower re-arrest and relapse rates compared to traditional correctional or treatment approaches. These findings are consistent with broader research on therapeutic communities, which have demonstrated efficacy in reducing substance use and criminal behavior when programs are long-term and peer-led (De Leon, 2000; Magura, 2007). Importantly, the voluntary and residential nature of Delancey Street enhances treatment engagement and creates a container for sustained personal transformation (Borkman et al., 1998; Schlosser, 2005).
> Critiques. However, the model is not without limitations and critiques (Magura, 2007; Kelly & White, 2011). Critics argue that the confrontational style of peer feedback and the absence of clinical oversight may be problematic for individuals with co-occurring mental health disorders or histories of trauma (Mitchell et al., 2001; De Leon, 2000). The program’s abstention from psychiatric medication or harm-reduction strategies may also limit accessibility for some populations (Magura, 2007; Kelly & White, 2011). Moreover, the emphasis on conformity and hierarchical progression can raise ethical questions about autonomy and emotional safety in peer-led environments (Borkman et al., 1998; Schlosser, 2005).

> Efficacy and Outcome Research. AA’s effectiveness has been studied extensively, though often with methodological complexity due to its decentralized structure and ethical constraints on randomized trials. Project MATCH, one of the most influential studies on treatment matching, found that individuals who attended AA frequently had better drinking outcomes, particularly among those with higher levels of alcohol dependence (Project MATCH Research Group, 1997). Longitudinal data from Vaillant (2003, 2012) and others have shown that consistent participation in AA (defined as weekly or more frequent meeting attendance) is positively correlated with sustained abstinence, sometimes even outperforming professional treatment modalities. Kelly et al. (2020), in a comprehensive Cochrane review, concluded that AA and Twelve Step Facilitation (TSF) interventions produce comparable or superior outcomes to cognitive-behavioral therapy in promoting long-term abstinence.
> Nevertheless, rates of sustained sobriety vary widely. A review by Kaskutas (2009) suggests that only about 20–25% of AA newcomers remain continuously abstinent after one year, though those who stay for 90 days or more show higher success rates. Factors influencing retention include social support, spirituality, and congruence with personal values.
> Critiques of the AA Model. Despite its wide reach, AA has faced substantial critique from academic, medical, and advocacy communities. Chief among these is the religious or spiritual framing of recovery, which some view as exclusionary or ineffective for secular individuals (Humphreys, 2004). While AA emphasizes that “God” may be interpreted broadly, critics argue that theistic assumptions are embedded in its core language and structure. Others have challenged the emphasis on powerlessness, arguing that this stance may undermine self-efficacy and reinforce helplessness (Peele, 2001).
> Another critique centers on the lack of formal accountability or clinical oversight. AA’s peer-led structure, while empowering in many ways, raises concerns about the potential spread of misinformation, variable group quality, and the lack of evidence-based psychiatric support for co-occurring disorders (Room & Greenfield, 2013). Feminist and trauma-informed critics have also highlighted that the moral inventory and confession aspects may retraumatize individuals with histories of abuse (Kasl, 1992). Despite these critiques, AA offers an excellent example of  the application of several of the factors that lead to recovery discussed in the previous section. AA uses social support, spiritual awakening, and shift in social identity to help addicts make the leap from addiction to sobriety. Other organizations offer a different mix of factors.
> SMART Recovery
> SMART Recovery (Self-Management and Recovery Training) is a non-profit, evidence-informed addiction recovery program grounded in cognitive-behavioral and motivational enhancement principles (Horvath & Yeterian, 2012; SMART Recovery, 2023). Developed in 1994 as a secular alternative to the 12-step model popularized by Alcoholics Anonymous (AA), SMART Recovery is based on the belief that individuals struggling with addiction can achieve abstinence through the cultivation of self-reliance, emotional regulation, and rational decision-making (Brooks & Penn, 2003; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). Unlike AA, which posits addiction as a lifelong disease necessitating spiritual surrender, SMART Recovery treats addiction as a maladaptive behavior that can be changed through structured interventions and personal empowerment (Kelly et al., 2020; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012).
> Theoretical Foundations and Core Principles. SMART Recovery is primarily rooted in principles derived from cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT), and Motivational Interviewing (MI) (Beck et al., 1993; Ellis, 1962; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). These frameworks collectively emphasize the role of distorted thinking, emotional dysregulation, and ambivalence in the maintenance of addictive behaviors (Beck et al., 1993; Miller & Rollnick, 2012). REBT, developed by Albert Ellis, is particularly influential in SMART Recovery’s emphasis on identifying and disputing irrational beliefs that perpetuate substance use (Ellis, 1962; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). In contrast to deterministic views of addiction, SMART Recovery promotes the notion that individuals possess agency and can change their behavior through conscious effort and psychological restructuring (Beck et al., 1993; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012).
> At the heart of the SMART Recovery model lies the 4-Point Program, which serves as a flexible framework rather than a rigid sequence (SMART Recovery, 2023; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). The four points include: (1) Building and Maintaining Motivation, (2) Coping with Urges, (3) Managing Thoughts, Feelings, and Behaviors, and (4) Living a Balanced Life (SMART Recovery, 2023). These points guide participants through a process of self-exploration, cognitive reframing, and lifestyle redesign (Horvath & Yeterian, 2012; Brooks & Penn, 2003). Importantly, SMART Recovery does not adopt the disease model of addiction but views addictive behaviors as learned and reinforced patterns that can be unlearned and replaced (Beck et al., 1993; SMART Recovery, 2023).
> Program Structure and Tools. SMART Recovery meetings, facilitated by trained volunteers or professionals, are designed to be interactive and participatory (SMART Recovery, 2023; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). Participants are encouraged to share their experiences, engage in collaborative problem-solving, and practice self-monitoring (Brooks & Penn, 2003; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). A typical meeting may involve reviewing tools such as cost-benefit analysis (CBA), ABC (Activating event, Beliefs, Consequences) worksheets, or the Hierarchy of Values exercise—each aimed at enhancing insight and promoting behavioral change (Ellis, 1962; Beck et al., 1993; SMART Recovery, 2023).
> One of the model’s signature tools is the ABC analysis derived from REBT, which helps participants identify the irrational beliefs that mediate between a triggering event and maladaptive behavior (Ellis, 1962; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). For example, a person who relapses after an argument may, through ABC analysis, uncover the belief “I can’t stand being criticized,” and work toward replacing it with a more adaptive thought such as “I don’t like criticism, but I can handle it” (Ellis, 1962; Beck et al., 1993). Such cognitive restructuring is central to the SMART philosophy, which holds that emotional resilience and behavioral control are outcomes of more rational thinking (Beck et al., 1993; SMART Recovery, 2023).
> Additionally, the CBA tool encourages individuals to weigh the short-term and long-term consequences of using versus not using substances (Beck et al., 1993; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). This helps to destabilize the romanticized view of substance use and reinforce more reality-based motivations for abstinence (Bogenschutz et al., 2020; SMART Recovery, 2023). The emphasis on skill acquisition and self-efficacy aligns SMART Recovery closely with contemporary cognitive-behavioral treatment modalities (Beck et al., 1993; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012).
> Unlike 12-step programs, which place a strong emphasis on surrendering to a higher power and accepting powerlessness, SMART Recovery encourages individuals to develop a sense of personal control and responsibility (Brooks & Penn, 2003; Kelly et al., 2020). While spirituality is not excluded, it is not framed as a necessary component for recovery (SMART Recovery, 2023; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). This makes the model especially appealing to individuals who are secular, skeptical of religious doctrine, or disillusioned with traditional recovery paradigms (Zemore et al., 2018; Brooks & Penn, 2003).
> Empirical Support and Comparative Effectiveness
> Although SMART Recovery is not as extensively studied as 12-step programs, a growing body of literature supports its efficacy (Kelly et al., 2020; Zemore et al., 2018). In a comparative study by Zemore et al. (2018), individuals who participated in alternative mutual-help groups like SMART Recovery reported comparable improvements in abstinence, psychological well-being, and quality of life to those attending AA (Zemore et al., 2018). Importantly, these benefits were more pronounced among individuals who preferred secular recovery models or who expressed discomfort with the spiritual components of 12-step programs (Zemore et al., 2018; Brooks & Penn, 2003).
> Another randomized controlled trial by Bogenschutz et al. (2020) found that participation in SMART Recovery was associated with increased self-efficacy, improved emotional regulation, and reduced substance use over a 6-month follow-up period (Bogenschutz et al., 2020). These findings support the theoretical premise that enhancing cognitive and emotional skills can yield clinically meaningful outcomes (Beck et al., 1993; Horvath & Yeterian, 2012). Furthermore, the flexible, self-directed nature of SMART Recovery aligns with contemporary understandings of recovery as a non-linear and individualized process (Laudet & White, 2010; Bogenschutz et al., 2020).
> However, it is important to note that SMART Recovery may not be effective for all individuals (Kelly et al., 2020; Brooks & Penn, 2003). Some participants may struggle with the cognitive demands of the program, particularly those with co-occurring mental health disorders, cognitive impairments, or lower levels of education (Laudet & White, 2010; Kelly et al., 2020). Moreover, the absence of a strong community or spiritual framework may render the program less effective for individuals who benefit from the communal and existential support found in 12-step groups (Kelly et al., 2020; Zemore et al., 2018). Thus, clinical practitioners must carefully assess client preferences, values, and cognitive capacities when recommending a recovery model (Bogenschutz et al., 2020; Laudet & White, 2010).
> Clinical and Philosophical Implications. SMART Recovery’s emphasis on autonomy, rationality, and self-determination introduces important philosophical and clinical questions about the nature of addiction and the path to recovery (Ellis, 1962; Beck et al., 1993). By rejecting the disease model and affirming the possibility of recovery without spiritual surrender, SMART Recovery challenges dominant narratives in the addiction treatment landscape (Horvath & Yeterian, 2012; SMART Recovery, 2023). It re-centers recovery on the development of agency, psychological insight, and adaptive behavior (Beck et al., 1993; Brooks & Penn, 2003). As such, SMART utilizes shift in identity, psychotherapy and social support as its main tools in helping addicts-in-recovery to deepen in their sovereignty. This last factor, social support, is not as strong in SMART Recovery as it is in AA.
> Recovery Dharma
> Recovery Dharma is a peer-led, non-theistic addiction recovery community that utilizes Buddhist principles and meditation practices as the foundation for healing from substance use and compulsive behaviors (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021). The model emerged in 2019 from a restructuring of Refuge Recovery, itself an earlier Buddhist-based recovery approach founded in 2014 by Noah Levine (Levine, 2014; Recovery Dharma, 2020). Following a leadership crisis in Refuge Recovery, the community reorganized democratically to emphasize decentralized, inclusive governance, giving rise to what is now known as Recovery Dharma (Larkin et al., 2021; Recovery Dharma, 2020). This model offers a distinctive alternative to conventional programs like Alcoholics Anonymous by grounding recovery not in surrender to a higher power, but in mindfulness, ethical living, and self-investigation (Monteiro, Musten, & Compson, 2015; Kelly & Greene, 2018).
> Theoretical foundations. Recovery Dharma is based on the Four Noble Truths and the Eightfold Path of Buddhism, which are adapted to address the specific suffering associated with addiction (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021). The First Truth identifies the presence of suffering, which in the context of addiction includes craving, shame, and disconnection (Levine, 2014; Marlatt, 2002). The Second Truth emphasizes that suffering is caused by craving and attachment, including the compulsive use of substances or behaviors to avoid pain or pursue pleasure (Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999; Recovery Dharma, 2020). The Third Truth asserts that freedom from suffering is possible, offering hope and self-efficacy to individuals who have felt powerless in their addiction (Monteiro et al., 2015; Kelly & Greene, 2018). The Fourth Truth, the path to liberation, is actualized through the Eightfold Path, which becomes a roadmap for ethical, mindful living and recovery (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021).
> The Eightfold Path in Recovery Dharma consists of right understanding, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, and concentration, all of which are interpreted through the lens of trauma-informed care and non-theistic spirituality (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Monteiro et al., 2015). Right understanding involves recognizing addiction as a form of suffering and cultivating insight into its causes, including trauma and systemic oppression (Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999; Larkin et al., 2021). Right intention fosters the development of compassion and non-harming toward self and others, counteracting the shame and self-blame common in addiction (Levine, 2014; Kelly & Greene, 2018). Right speech and action encourage honesty, accountability, and non-harming behavior, which are essential for rebuilding trust in relationships (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Monteiro et al., 2015). Right livelihood emphasizes ethical work and reducing exploitation, connecting recovery with broader social values (Marlatt, 2002; Recovery Dharma, 2020).
> Methods. Right effort, mindfulness, and concentration are particularly emphasized through the regular practice of meditation, which is a cornerstone of the Recovery Dharma model (Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999; Larkin et al., 2021). Members are encouraged to engage in daily mindfulness meditation to observe cravings, emotions, and thoughts without reacting to them impulsively (Monteiro et al., 2015; Recovery Dharma, 2020). This emphasis on direct experience and self-awareness distinguishes Recovery Dharma from more externally directed approaches to recovery, such as the 12-step model (Kelly & Greene, 2018; Levine, 2014). The cultivation of present-moment awareness helps participants develop greater emotional regulation and distress tolerance—skills that are predictive of sustained abstinence (Witkiewitz, Marlatt, & Walker, 2005; Larkin et al., 2021).
> A central practice in Recovery Dharma is inquiry, which involves a deep, structured examination of past behaviors, motivations, and relationships using Buddhist psychological frameworks (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Monteiro et al., 2015). Participants undertake a process of personal inventory, akin to Step Four in the 12-step model, but without invoking a higher power or confession to others (Kelly & Greene, 2018; Levine, 2014). Instead, the emphasis is on self-compassionate awareness and letting go of fixed identities, particularly the addict identity that can perpetuate shame and limit growth (Larkin et al., 2021; Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999). Inquiry is typically supported by peer-led groups, which provide relational accountability without hierarchical sponsorship structures (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Monteiro et al., 2015).
> Recovery Dharma meetings are peer-facilitated, secular, and consensus-based, reflecting the Buddhist value of sangha, or spiritual community (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021). Meetings often include meditation, readings from the Recovery Dharma book, shared reflections, and compassionate listening, creating a container that supports both personal and collective healing (Monteiro et al., 2015; Marlatt, 2002). The model emphasizes inclusivity and accessibility, with explicit commitments to anti-racism, gender inclusion, and trauma awareness (Recovery Dharma, 2020; Larkin et al., 2021). These commitments reflect an understanding that addiction is both a personal and social condition, shaped by interlocking systems of oppression, marginalization, and trauma (Witkiewitz et al., 2005; Marlatt, 2002).
> The empirical base for Buddhist-informed recovery is still emerging, but promising evidence supports the efficacy of mindfulness-based approaches for SUDs (Witkiewitz et al., 2014; Garland et al., 2010). Mindfulness-based relapse prevention (MBRP), which shares significant overlap with Recovery Dharma, has been shown to reduce substance use, improve emotional regulation, and decrease relapse risk in multiple randomized controlled trials (Witkiewitz et al., 2014; Bowen et al., 2009). These outcomes support the core assertion of Recovery Dharma that mindfulness and non-reactivity are protective factors against addiction (Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999; Garland et al., 2010). Moreover, the incorporation of ethical living and community support may enhance engagement and retention compared to purely individualistic interventions (Monteiro et al., 2015; Larkin et al., 2021).
> Critiques of Recovery Dharma. Critics of Recovery Dharma and similar models caution that the program’s cognitive and introspective demands may not be suitable for all individuals, particularly those with severe psychiatric comorbidities or cognitive impairments (Kelly & Greene, 2018; Witkiewitz et al., 2005). The lack of a centralized structure or formal treatment professionals may also be a barrier for those who prefer clinician-led interventions (Larkin et al., 2021; Marlatt, 2002). However, its accessibility, emphasis on agency, and holistic view of recovery make Recovery Dharma a powerful alternative for individuals disillusioned with traditional or theistic models (Monteiro et al., 2015; Levine, 2014).
> Summary. Philosophically, Recovery Dharma reframes addiction not as a disease or a moral failing, but as a natural human response to suffering and disconnection (Marlatt, 2002; Recovery Dharma, 2020). This view reduces stigma and opens space for compassion, both toward oneself and others in the recovery process (Monteiro et al., 2015; Larkin et al., 2021). By emphasizing impermanence, interdependence, and self-inquiry, Recovery Dharma encourages individuals to move beyond rigid identities and cultivate freedom from conditioned patterns (Levine, 2014; Marlatt & Kristeller, 1999). As such, it represents not only a recovery program, but a comprehensive spiritual and psychological path that continues to grow in relevance as recovery culture becomes increasingly pluralistic (Kelly & Greene, 2018; Witkiewitz et al., 2014). When asking which of the several factors that support abstinence discussed earlier is used in the Recovery Dharma program, we see meditation, shift in identity and social support as prominent parts of their process.
> Delancey Street
> The Delancey Street Foundation is one of the most well-known and long-standing residential self-help organizations for individuals recovering from substance use, criminal behavior, and poverty (Mitchell et al., 2001; Schlosser, 2005). Founded in 1971 in San Francisco by Mimi Silbert and John Maher, Delancey Street operates as a structured, peer-run therapeutic community (TC) grounded in principles of mutual responsibility, social enterprise, and long-term behavioral transformation (Silbert & Pines, 1983; De Leon, 2000). Unlike conventional addiction treatment programs that rely on clinical professionals, Delancey Street is entirely staffed and operated by its residents, who enter voluntarily and commit to staying a minimum of two years without external funding or medication-assisted treatment (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Mitchell et al., 2001). The model emphasizes personal accountability, meaningful work, community immersion, and a shift in social identity as the primary agents of change (Schlosser, 2005; De Leon, 2000).
> Methods. The program is based on the therapeutic community (TC) model, which conceptualizes addiction not as an isolated disease, but as a symptom of broader deficits in social functioning, identity formation, and self-regulation (De Leon, 2000; Magura, 2007). Delancey Street applies this framework by creating a total environment in which every element—from chores to job training—is viewed as part of the therapeutic process (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Mitchell et al., 2001). Central to this model is the notion that the “community as method” approach can re-socialize individuals who have been marginalized or criminalized due to their addiction and behavior (De Leon, 2000; Borkman et al., 1998). This approach relies heavily on peer feedback, role modeling, and experiential learning rather than formal psychotherapy or pharmacological treatment (Magura, 2007; Schlosser, 2005).
> Delancey Street differs significantly from other recovery models in its emphasis on self-governance and mutual teaching (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Mitchell et al., 2001). New residents are paired with senior peers who mentor them through daily responsibilities, life skills, and behavioral expectations (De Leon, 2000; Borkman et al., 1998). These mentor-mentee relationships are seen as essential for breaking entrenched patterns of dependency and instilling values such as discipline, integrity, and delayed gratification (Schlosser, 2005; Silbert & Pines, 1983). The program does not allow for drug replacement therapies or psychiatric medications, instead prioritizing immersive behavioral restructuring through communal living and labor (Mitchell et al., 2001; Magura, 2007).
> Residents at Delancey Street engage in full-time participation in social enterprises run by the organization, including moving companies, restaurants, catering services, and construction businesses (Schlosser, 2005; Silbert & Pines, 1983). These businesses serve dual purposes: generating revenue for the organization and providing residents with real-world vocational training and work habits (De Leon, 2000; Mitchell et al., 2001). Each resident progresses through a meritocratic system in which they take on increasing levels of responsibility, both in business operations and in community governance (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Borkman et al., 1998). This structure reinforces the development of self-esteem, economic competence, and leadership capacity—core dimensions of the program’s definition of recovery (De Leon, 2000; Magura, 2007).
> Theoretical model. The theoretical underpinnings of Delancey Street are influenced by behavioral psychology, humanistic education, and social learning theory (De Leon, 2000; Borkman et al., 1998). The community acts as a corrective emotional experience in which maladaptive behaviors are openly confronted through group feedback and modeled alternatives (Mitchell et al., 2001; Magura, 2007). Negative behaviors, such as manipulation, dishonesty, or entitlement, are addressed through peer-led interventions and accountability structures, not punitive measures (Schlosser, 2005; Silbert & Pines, 1983). Residents are expected to “act as if” they are already responsible, capable individuals—a concept drawn from cognitive-behavioral and existential traditions that suggest identity can be reshaped through disciplined performance (De Leon, 2000; Borkman et al., 1998).
> Unlike 12-step programs or clinical treatment models, Delancey Street refrains from labeling its participants as addicts or alcoholics (Schlosser, 2005; Silbert & Pines, 1983). The focus is not on pathology but on potential: every resident is treated as a student capable of change and excellence (Mitchell et al., 2001; Borkman et al., 1998). This strengths-based approach fosters dignity, agency, and intrinsic motivation, which are critical for sustaining long-term behavioral change (Magura, 2007; De Leon, 2000). By providing opportunities for leadership, education, and entrepreneurship, the program reframes recovery as a process of social reintegration and self-actualization (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Schlosser, 2005).
> Empirical research. Empirical research on Delancey Street is limited but promising, with several qualitative and retrospective studies documenting low recidivism rates, sustained abstinence, and vocational reintegration among graduates (Mitchell et al., 2001; Borkman et al., 1998). A study by Mitchell and colleagues (2001) found that among criminal justice-involved individuals, participation in Delancey Street was associated with significantly lower re-arrest and relapse rates compared to traditional correctional or treatment approaches. These findings are consistent with broader research on therapeutic communities, which have demonstrated efficacy in reducing substance use and criminal behavior when programs are long-term and peer-led (De Leon, 2000; Magura, 2007). Importantly, the voluntary and residential nature of Delancey Street enhances treatment engagement and creates a container for sustained personal transformation (Borkman et al., 1998; Schlosser, 2005).
> Critiques. However, the model is not without limitations and critiques (Magura, 2007; Kelly & White, 2011). Critics argue that the confrontational style of peer feedback and the absence of clinical oversight may be problematic for individuals with co-occurring mental health disorders or histories of trauma (Mitchell et al., 2001; De Leon, 2000). The program’s abstention from psychiatric medication or harm-reduction strategies may also limit accessibility for some populations (Magura, 2007; Kelly & White, 2011). Moreover, the emphasis on conformity and hierarchical progression can raise ethical questions about autonomy and emotional safety in peer-led environments (Borkman et al., 1998; Schlosser, 2005).
> Summary. Despite these concerns, Delancey Street remains a unique and influential recovery model that challenges conventional paradigms of addiction treatment (Silbert & Pines, 1983; Kelly & White, 2011). It rejects both the medicalization and moralization of addiction in favor of a communal, educational, and developmental framework (De Leon, 2000; Borkman et al., 1998). By placing recovery within the context of citizenship, labor, and mutual respect, Delancey Street provides an alternative vision for healing that emphasizes growth over diagnosis, and contribution over compliance (Schlosser, 2005; Silbert & Pines, 1983).
> Philosophically, Delancey Street blurs the boundaries between rehabilitation, education, and social justice (De Leon, 2000; Kelly & White, 2011).
